Digital Library Federation
Framework for a Distributed Open Digital Library
Prepared by the DODL Initiative Committee[1]

27 May 2003


DLF proposes to move digital library development to a new phase, a phase characterized by increasing contributions of content, a deeper context for sharing that content and a more robust architecture for supporting digital services through the creation of a Distributed Open Digital Library (DODL).


The Digital Library Federation has a strategic mission to foster and leverage digital library activity among its members and in consort with other allied organizations. Since its inception, it has provided leadership for digital library development, served as a catalyst in the creation of innovative information services and organizations, and provided a venue and context for learning within the profession.


DLF’s mission further recognizes that the digital library extends the breadth and scale of scholarly and cultural evidence and thus supports innovative research and life-long learning. (A sampling of such research and learning is presented in the final section of this document.) To accomplish this mission, it mediates between diverse and distributed information resources on the one hand and a changing range of user communities on the other. In this capacity, it will provide a multi-institutional framework in which individual research libraries will create their own collections of digital objects for scholarship and teaching but, working as the DLF, will establish a complementary digital library service environment” – that is, a networked, online information space in which users can discover, locate, acquire access to and, increasingly, use information. (DLF Strategic Plan 2000)


Early development of this “online information space” by DLF members has focused on projects and the development of production processes and systems in the first phase of DLF’s operations. A rich array of content has been created during these developmental years, typically focusing on the needs of a particular community and a particular type of material. Through collaboration, specifications for digital library components have been pursued and new tools developed for broader use. Yet, the landscape of digital library development in 2003 remains largely focused on institution-specific methodologies and surface-level sharing of digital assets.[2] We have not yet created a digital library resource that truly begins to reflect the scope, depth and functionality of a large research library.


A Case for a Federated Digital Library

Libraries are collaborative by nature. Shared cataloging is a striking early example. This interdependence has been extended through the linking of content between and among libraries. OAI efforts have also prompted a number of venues through which metadata can be shared and harvested for new services. The creation of a Distributed Open Digital Library (DODL) by DLF would further enhance sharing in ways that move significantly deeper than the status quo in terms of function, service, re-use of content, and library interdependency.


The current information landscape is dotted with rich content silos that can be visited but which resist innovative distributed recombination and re-use. This thwarts the integration of remotely held content into local library services, courseware systems, and scholarly desktop data-analysis tools. Fundamental needs of users of the distributed open digital library will be better served by exploring a collaboratively built, federated digital library to which all participants contribute content and from which libraries can draw files into local collections for innovative use as the needs of local users dictate.


Such a federated digital library may provide a common interface. Within the parameters of content rights, it would enable librarians and end-users alike to download “digital use copies” files as malleable objects for local re-combinations, to be enriched with context, crafted for specific audiences, and unified in appearance and function.


Goals

The goals of the Distributed Open Digital Library (DODL) include:


  • To create a framework for shared digital library content, tools, and services among the DLF membership and broader community
  • To catalyze collaboration among digital library stakeholders in the development of rich content and innovative services
  • To develop an extensible architecture to leverage existing digital library assets, enable the development of layered services, and explore models for managing digital content rights in a collaborative environment
  • To generate, facilitate and evaluate use of digital library content and services among our users
  • To engage potential funding agencies in this effort

In short, the DODL is intended to enable better, as well as broader, use of digital assets.


Populating the Distributed Open Digital Library


The vision and purpose of the DLF since its founding has been to enable a large scale federated digital library that advances the ongoing collaborative efforts of research libraries to deliver the rich mosaic of their complementary holdings to scholars. Much has been accomplished by DLF members, both separately and collectively, in recent years. DLF work has included conversion of large content collections and development of standards, best practices, and the Open Archives Initiative, an important foundation in federating access to digital library content.


The DODL initiative takes the next major step forward in pursuit of the DLF vision. At its core is design and development to enable true sharing and use of digital library resources. It is also a vehicle for increasing the amount and value of DLF content contribution in a sustainable manner. When DLF members can truly share digital resources, contribution of digital content conveys the benefits of cooperative collection development: each library’s contribution is repaid by complete access to the larger collection. If the value of the collection is sufficiently attractive, digital contribution becomes a means of acquisition, potentially sustained with library operating funds.


The model of coordinated collection development has always worked best when it naturally builds on institutional collecting policies and needs. While “altruistic” cooperative collection development is unrealistic—since libraries are locally funded for their own institutional objectives—various forms of value-added coordination and project activity have created a national mosaic of research collections that is far richer than insulated individual collecting could engender. We can now take this effort further in the digital library arena, creating a distributed open digital collection that in total is greater than the sum of its contributed parts. Collaborations within DLF, even as small as dyads and triads, can bring the same kind of value-added to DODL collection expansion. Each library serves the collective good by meeting local needs with either its own efforts or by limited collaborations, with the DODL technical and organizational framework providing guidance, but not controlling process.


There is already a rich array of digital content available from DLF libraries, but it is disjoined and episodic and often functions more as an exhibit for viewing than as a library for interrogating. In a recent study of ARL and DLF digital collections, M. Dalbello finds that these collections are much like museum exhibits, albeit somewhat rudimentary in terms of contextual presentation (Library Quarterly, forthcoming). She concludes that the current efforts can best be described by analogy to the development of museums and the early cabinets of curiosities. While her study admires the efforts of libraries to expand their role in presenting content to the public, as research librarians we must also be concerned that we are creating is the true analog of the research library—i.e., a digital library that makes rich research collections available for use.


Thus, the goal of the DODL is not to create another self-standing thematic presentation, but to bring a large amount of compelling and useful content into its realm – both on a short-term project basis and by encouraging a long-term pattern of collection contribution. The DODL Initiative Committee recommends to the DLF Executive Committee that it issue a call to DLF members to contribute content in accordance with the following recommendations:


  1. DODL resources must be shareable in the DODL environment through appropriate technical standards to be defined by a Technical Subcommittee. The objective for such standards is to be able to use DODL objects in the service layers described in the following section. With an emphasis on the user experience, the standards need to strike a balance between compatibility for ease of use and practicality to enable both near term adoption and the incorporation of existing corpora of material.
  2. DODL resources must be shareable through workable arrangements for access rights. Although it would be ideal, it is neither necessary nor realistic to require that all content in the DODL be essentially in the public domain or cleared for general public access. Part of the development of the DODL technical infrastructure requires means to authorize categories of users. This could include requiring a user to self-authorize by a “click through” agreement that use is for scholarly purposes, but will also require the ability to authorize categories of users by institutional affiliation, e.g., DLF member institutions or members of a particular scholarly association.
  3. DODL content must be of value to, at minimum, a large body of scholars and thus be material to which a majority of DLF members would desire access.
  4. DODL content should be in all formats – print, image, multi-media, numeric, etc. – appropriate to scholarly inquiry.

The DODL initiative seeks content contributions from its members that match one or more of the following categories:


  • Restructuring of appropriate existing digital collections to meet Criterion 1 (above). We want to take advantage of investments already made.
  • Collections developed in collaboration with a national or international community of scholars. Such collections may be highly focused to meet a recognized research need, such as the content of the Advanced Papyrological Information System (APIS). However, a much broader scope is also appropriate, such as the materials and projects registered under the umbrella of ECAI, the Electronic Cultural Atlas Initiative, a community of scholars using digital technology to present data through imagery and global mapping. Our objective would be to make DODL a meaningful resource for the partner community.
  • Collections developed collaboratively by two or more DLF members. These projects should demonstrate the value of resource sharing in the digital realm by assembling a body of materials enriched by their integration.

A Layered Service Model for Implementing the Distributed Open Digital Library


In the past few years, we have heard a great deal about a new “layered” service model.[3] This model would have libraries building highly customized local services on the base of content and services managed by third parties. The service model promises the same degree of flexibility, innovation, and customization that is associated with local service provision without sacrificing the economies that are achieved through the shared centralized model. It also promises to strengthen the library’s historic and essential mediating role between users and the information they seek. As such, it seems a natural fit with the aims of the DODL.


Figure 1 represents schematically how the DODL’s integration into the layered service model may be conceived. The figure depicts four digital object repositories. These are simply digital collections. The collections could comprise encoded texts (e.g. from the text centers at Virginia, Michigan, Stanford, and elsewhere), digital images (e.g. at Cornell, Yale, Minnesota, New York Public Library), research publications (e.g. in pre-print servers or institutional repositories at Cornell and MIT, respectively), sound recordings (Indiana), archival finding aids (Chicago), maps (Texas), and data collections (e.g. at Emory). And the collections could be managed by commercial or society publishers, by universities, research institutions, or by companies working in the entertainment industry, as well as by libraries.


Users can browse, search, and display the content of each of these repositories by visiting its website. Users can also build “higher-level services” based on the digital objects (the encoded, texts, faculty publications, digital images, sound recordings, etc) that the repositories contain. Higher-level services can use some or all of the digital objects within one or more of the repositories. Thus, a higher level service supporting study of the “American West” may refer to some objects in several underlying repositories (digital images of the West from an image repository, publications about the West from a pre-print server, encoded texts of works of western authors from the text repository)


Figure 1


Figure 1 also depicts several types of the higher level services that might be built directly on top of the repositories. For example:


  • A subject portal – allows users to search seamlessly across selected objects in underlying repositories; that is, to view as a single database the distributed publications, research datasets, and digital images that reside in the underlying repositories.
  • An archives portal integrates access to online finding aids developed and maintained by hundreds of libraries, archives, and special collections and will be referred to again later.
  • An alerting service notifies individual end users about additions to (and deletions from) repositories.
  • A print on demand and PDA delivery services reformat document like objects from underlying repositories and deliver them to a user’s palm top or to a local print on demand service that creates a perfect bound low-cost paperback edition.

Additional service scenarios are included in the final section of this document.


Some higher-level services are built on top of services that mediate or shape their interaction with underlying repositories. For example, authentication and commercial transaction services might intervene between the print on demand and PDA delivery service and the repositories that it is built upon. This would ensure, for example, that only fee-paying customers were able to print or deliver to their PDA’s the electronic editions of books and articles found in the underlying repositories. The figure also shows some other services that require three or more service strata.


The layered service model has enormous potential for libraries.


The DLF’s DODL initiative will leverage the wealth and breadth of its members’ digital collections and their technical capacity to practically evaluate the layered service model, to address the numerous implementation questions that surround its development and exploitation, and to assess the model’s potential costs to and benefits for academic libraries.


Planning the Distributed Open Digital Library: Next Steps


A DODL Initiative Committee has thus far explored the goals and scope of the proposed program. Further, it has articulated the framework (described above) for moving beyond existing surface-level and descriptive methodologies for sharing content. . This is necessarily an incremental approach; the intention is to achieve what is possible in the near term on a limited scale and build from there. Following are near-term steps:


  • The DLF Executive Committee will appoint a Technology Subcommittee to specify the necessary components of an extensible, layered architectural model, identifying what components exist and where development is necessary. The design will be peer reviewed. There will be a call for nominees and volunteers for this subcommittee. Outside participants will be considered as a means of opening the process.
  • DLF will commission a review of existing Open Archives Initiative content and services by an external consultant to inform the Technology Subcommittee’s deliberations. This review will identify areas of successful federation and promising developments.
  • The DODL Initiative Committee will put out calls for member participation through content, services, or other development. This solicitation, which will be informed by the specifications of the DODL Technology Subcommittee, will call for:
    • Digital content (already in digital form or to be converted)
    • Development, adaptation, and/or integration of services as identified by the Technology Subcommittee
    • Individuals or groups that would test designs, services, and instances of federated collections accessible by the services
    • Institutions willing and ready to work in groups of two or more institutions on content, services, integration, and/or tests
  • The DODL Initiative Committee will develop principles for coordinating and managing the DODL development process and identify necessary operational support for this early phase. These principles will form the basis for future discussion of a governance model for the DODL and will be circulated for review and comment.
  • The DLF Executive Committee will, as needs are identified, designate or hire additional project support to further the program development. A permanent steering committee will be created as the project transitions from the development stage.
  • DLF will make appropriate efforts toward fund-raising for DODL efforts.
  • The DODL Initiative Committee and DLF members will pursue outreach to related efforts and programs toward greater critical mass and breadth of materials and wider adoption of the DODL as a means for building and sharing collections.

Some Use Scenarios for the Distributed Open Digital Library


The DODL will be user-focused to provide rich and meaningful access to shared digital library content. Yet DODL will also be library-focused to achieve new efficiencies in digital library production, collection building, and services. The following scenarios illustrate some of the ways the DODL can serve both local and collective purposes. Examples of layered services, mechanisms for re-purposing content, and obstacles in the current environment are highlighted in these scenarios.


Scenario 1

Imagine you are teaching the Civil War, or you are a librarian crafting a collection in support of a seminar on that subject. You quickly discover via the Internet many books, images, letters, and manuscripts scattered across dozens of institutions. At present, all you can do is to scrutinize that data where it resides, in formats that the creator of the content determined. You can make a list of resources—a web bibliography—or write a description with hypertext links to the scattered collections, and your students can visit these other collections, akin to making museum fieldtrips. But that is all—a passive engagement with content rather than an active one. You cannot combine those scattered objects into something new, improved, and shaped for your local needs.


Twenty books in twenty different locations cannot easily be searched together, or enriched with information and design elements of value for a local project, or dropped into desktop software that may allow annotation by the user, or subjected to linguistic or statistical analysis that the original website does not support, or delivered in a format (Palm Pilot, E-book) that the producer did not think useful, or used in myriad other ways.


Libraries create high-quality digital masters for long-term preservation and re-use, but then typically expose only one view of a file to the user, in one particular search-and-display software package. Rather than offering a single view of a digital object that serves one type of need, while under-serving others, DODL may allow teachers, librarians, and other users options for re-shaping and combining content.


Scenario 2

Professor Sorenson prepares for a web-based discussion with students and scholars related to the Arthurian mythology. A broad-based search, using Z39.50, OAI-based, full-text, and multimedia-based techniques, across a federation of repositories results in thousands of potential resources. Using visualization and filtering tools, Professor Sorenson chooses a subset and a taxonomy for organization and categorization of the digital content.


The web-based cohort interacts in real-time, proposes alternate organization models, and creates individual, customized e-portfolios, sometimes accessing materials that Professor Sorenson had considered "secondary" in importance. One student creates a blog that focuses on unexamined connections between T.H. White’s The Once and Future King, a song from the musical Camelot, the movie Excalibur, and John F. Kennedy’s presidency. In many cases, objects are re-purposed and manipulated into new learning objects for subsequent use.


Some of these new objects are deposited into an institutional repository as a bitstream. Professor Sorenson’s institution has defined the policies and processes for such deposit, ensuring that the content will be preserved and be seamlessly ported into electronic courseware, e-publishing, and digital library systems for subsequent, and sometimes unanticipated, use through various interfaces and devices. DODL creates the opportunity for Sorenson and students to use resources elsewhere and to make their work product discoverable and available to others.


Scenario 3

There is considerable interest in recreation of medieval performance, including not only music and drama, but also narration. Although we have manuscript documentation of such medieval creative work, the core expression was, by definition, the performance. Scholars around the world are working on such recreations, and can now (at least in theory) share them digitally so that they can be studied. A collection of digital performance pieces could be used in segments, recombined, analyzed, compared, and commented upon, in the same manner that scholars share text that they are working on. Typically such content is now presented in individual, contextual Websites, not offered up as a “database” for use. Incorporating such objects into the DODL, however, could make them widely available for much deeper use.


Scenario 4

Imagine a musicologist investigating the simultaneous use of jazz idioms and stylistic elements by French classical musicians of the first half of the 20th century and the use by American jazz musicians of French musical elements in the same period. In addition to the core musical elements such as harmonic structures, use of specific chords, rhythmic patterns, formal structures, distinctive decorative elements, modal melodies, and more or less clear quotation, there are social, economic, and perhaps even political questions. Among the sub-topics arising in this research are those of racial boundaries, exoticism as an element of popular taste, the transmission of stylistic elements among musical communities, and compositional practice and intentions. Ellington, Ravel, and Stravinsky will figure prominently in this inquiry.


In order to pursue this line of research on the core musical question, the scholar will need to go much further than the well known works from the repertories. She will have to convert exemplars of the styles to transposable, numeric “signatures” in digital form. Then, she will compare her signature elements to thousands of digital excerpts taken from many collections. Automatic searches with the specifications of composers, performers, groups, styles, critics, performing locations, and titles of works will help her search the contents of digitized newspapers of the period, as well as magazines, histories of music, memoirs and collections of letters, and archival documents of the key composers and musicians. DODL makes the discovery and use of these various resources practical.


Scenario 5

DODL can have a role in the production and distribution of scholarly monographs that responds to the following three-headed conundrum:


1) Monographs continue to play an important role in the recruitment, retention, and reward of scholars working in several disciplines.

2) Library demand for monographs is declining. A publisher that 15 years ago could count on US libraries buying 1,500 copies of a new monograph, might sell only 200 or 300 copies of a new monograph that is published today.

3) Scholarly publishers have responded to diminishing demand for monographs by drastically curtailing the number of new titles that they publish annually. The impact is particularly hard-felt on scholars in disciplines where monograph publication is a pre-requisite for tenured (even tenure-track) jobs. The cycle, if unbroken, becomes a vicious one, yet libraries, publishers, and faculty, alike hesitate to break it.


To deal with this challenge, DODL might work with presses to explore new models of monograph production and distribution models that enable libraries to reduce their dependence on redundant print acquisitions without undermining publisher revenues, the monograph publication process that is so critical to academic advancement, or patron’s access to monographic materials. One such model sees publishers making monographs available digitally as open repositories (not free, necessarily, but open) and printing only a limited number of hard and paperback editions on demand. DODL could be the locus and pioneer effort for such repositories.


The terms of digital distribution would need to be agreed with the publishers individually but should be designed to help the publisher recoup revenues loss from substantially reduced hard copy library sales. Various distribution scenarios could be offered as a fee-based service with a portion of the fee transmitted as a royalty to the publisher. Thus, the user might select from a progressively priced set of choices (somewhat like ebrary): read online, download some or all to a handheld device, print low-quality but perfect bound paperback copy locally, purchase hardcopy print edition from publisher or bookseller.


The model is essentially layered. Such a model, which DODL could support, may offer hope to:

· That part of the scholarly community that relies professionally on the monograph’s continued existence (university presses could grow monograph lists without requiring equal or greater growth in hard-copy sales);

· Libraries (that need to provide access to comprehensive collections but cannot do so by locally to acquiring, owning, and managing all of the print and digital items in them); and

· University presses and scholarly societies whose operations, though vital to scholarly communications, are not easily sustained by the current model of monograph production and distribution.
