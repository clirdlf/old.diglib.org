Digital Library Federation
Summary of Activity
March 1999 - June 1999

The following summary highlights DLF activity since the last DLF Steering Committee on March 19, 1999

Discipline-based digital libraries

Academic Image Exchange

In January, DLF convened a meeting to explore ways of using digital libraries to enhance the quality of art history teaching and research in the nation's colleges and universities. Art history faculty, visual resources librarians, and representatives of the College Art Association participated in the meeting. Participants are now proceeding with the development of prototype mechanism, called an image exchange. The facility would enable scholars to share with each other images, to which they own the rights, of works needed for teaching and research. Since March, participants in the projects have opened discussions of the development of the image exchange through formal presentations at the CNI spring meeting (see Part G) and at a regional meeting of the Visual Resources Association, and through a variety of informal presentations with interested organizations such as AMICO and art history publishers. In addition, DLF contracted with Robert Baron, an independent art historian and consultant with expertise in information technology and intellectual property matters, to serve as project manager. The Image Exchange organizing group met at Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh on May 22-23 to design and to begin constructing the prototype system. With the help of CMU computer scientists, the group settled on a comprehensive set of features for the Exchange (see Part H). It is now at work developing a set of these features, including a concordance of images in key art and architectural history textbooks, to comprise the prototype Image Exchange. Details of funding models for the Exchange are being fleshed out, a planning grant proposal for approximately $50,000 is being prepared to cover costs of the prototype development, and a generic grant proposal is being outlined to support the full development of the Exchange.

Social Science Data Archives Workshop

Susan Rosenblatt submitted a full report of the proceedings of the workshop held in January (see http://www.diglib.org/ssdaresults.htm). From the proceedings of the workshop, the report identifies a rich agenda for action including the following, which deserve the attention of DLF, its partners, and allies:

  • The use of numeric data in the social science curriculum appears to be expanding rapidly. This expansion draws attention to the limited availability of much-needed resources, including skilled staff and the infrastructure to disseminate SGML and XML encoded documentation. It requires library and other institutional leaders to be better informed about quantitative service needs, and it demands careful strategic planning for research and pedagogical needs within and across disciplines.
  • Independent, uncoordinated, and duplicative work is underway on nearly every campus developing and deploying easy to use mechanisms for faculty and students to use key datasets. Faculty participants emphasized that these access mechanisms must be designed to enable scientific replication of data analysis. Workshop participants held out the possibility that a centrally administered master plan could be devised for systematically dividing the labor of developing access tools among a variety of campuses. Gary King of Harvard University offered a different and compelling model of cooperation based on contributions of work in a common but distributed framework of data repositories and tools, which he calls the Virtual Data Center.
  • The development and deployment of a new SGML-based standard for documenting datasets in codebooks will require campus investments, offer opportunities for converting existing codebooks to digital form, and create the need for research to understand how online codebooks will relate to other discovery and access tools for numeric data.
  • Preservation strategies are needed to address the deterioration of codebooks and the dissemination of data products that bundle together and tightly integrate data and the tools for extracting and analyzing them.
  • Tools and other facilities are needed to help users understand and deal with comparability across related datasets.
  • Cataloging of datasets has been sorely neglected.
  • There is an opportunity for the development of consortia (or the use of existing consortia) to negotiate the purchase and licensing of critical and expensive datasets.

Digital Library Services: Digitizing and other production services

Guides to Quality in Visual Resource Imaging

This project is designed to document the objective measures of image qualities, such as color, tone, resolution, etc. and how they can be controlled in various aspects of an imaging process. The authors commissioned to produce the five guides are scheduled to submit their work on June 30 for editing and publication in early Fall. For additional details, see http://www.diglib.org/initfuncimage.htm.

Invitational workshop on technical metadata elements for image files

The National Information Standards Organization (NISO), the Council on Library and Information Resources (CLIR) and the Research Libraries Group (RLG) sponsored an invitational workshop on April 18th and 19th in Washington DC. The purpose of the workshop was to examine technical information needed to manage and use digital still images that reproduce a variety of pictures, documents and artifacts. Donald Waters was on the organizing committee for the workshop. Sixty individuals with a wide range of diverse interests and perspectives on the problem of metadata information attended the meeting. DLF partner and allied institutions were well represented among the attendees, who included libraries, universities, museums, archives, the digital library community, the government, and the digital imaging vendor community.

The meeting was facilitated by Jennifer Trant of the Art Museum Image Consortium. Howard Besser, a member of the organizing committee, delivered the keynote remarks and was followed by speakers who reported on metadata initiatives at the Library of Congress, the Research Libraries Group, the Art Museum Image Consortium, and the Making of America II project. In the deliberations that followed, the participants reached agreement on a variety of issues including the following:

  • a preliminary list of technical metadata elements
  • the use of industry standard metrics, where they exist, for assessing images (for example, tone, color, ICC profiles)
  • the need to develop methods of pointing at external test charts
  • a requirement for mechanisms enabling the metadata associated with an image to persist through various transformations.

The draft report can be found at http://www.niso.org/image.html.

Digital Library Services: Access management services

Developing systems of authorization

Under DLF auspices, the University of California, Columbia University, JSTOR, and OCLC are developing a protocol that will enable an information resource provider to verify that a user bearing a digital certificate has authority from a home institution to use a requested resource. The prototype system being developed combines the use of X.509 digital certificates for authentication with a directory service providing authorization to licensed resources based on user attributes. The first phase of work on the project was presented at the CNI Spring Meeting in Washington, D.C. in April of this year (see Part F). Just after CNI, project participants met and outlined a work plan for a second phase of development work.

The group focused on the need to recruit additional participants for further testing and development of the protocol. They devised a strategy to engage additional campus participants by aiming not only at library needs but also at campus technology organizations. The use of digital certificates is becoming part of campus technology infrastructure generally as a secure means for authorization to a range of campus systems and resources. DLF is therefore working in conjunction with CREN to develop a paper of frequently asked questions and answers about the use of digital certificates that will be targeted generally to senior campus administrators. In addition, project participants presented the project to the IT directors attending the spring meeting of the Common Solutions Group. In early June, project members also attended a meeting of the CIC's Research Projects Group charged with making recommendations to the Chief Information Officers of that consortium. As a result of these various efforts, several additional institutions have been identified as potential participants in the project.

In order to engage additional vendors in the project, Columbia and CDL are identifying vendors with whom they currently have agreements for access to licensed resources. Administrators of the Joint Information Systems Committee in the U.K., who are closely watching this project, have suggested several vendors who are working with them on authorizations systems.

Other work currently underway as part of the second phase of the project includes a draft digital library authentication and authorization architecture statement that will be presented to the IETF later this year for consideration as a Request for Comment (RFC). In addition, publications are being prepared to communicate the results of the project within various interested communities, and presentations are being proposed for the Fall CNI meeting as well as the EDUCAUSE meeting.

Digital Library Services: Preservation services

Institutional policies and practices for the long-term maintenance of digital materials

A joint RLG/DLF initiative, launched following the March meeting of the DLF Steering Committee, is taking a task force approach to advance our collective interest in and understanding of digital preservation policy and practice. Few institutions have formal policies in place but many institutions have made commitments to preserve digital materials and are actively engaged in operational practices supporting the handling and long-term maintenance of such materials. The goal of the task force is to identify and document these practices so that DLF and RLG can identify what kinds of technical, economic, and organizational barriers institutions are presently confronting and what kinds of actions are needed to lower those barriers.

The task group will gather and analyze existing digital preservation policies and practice descriptions for the following three classes of electronic materials:

  • Institutional Records in Digital Form (e.g., electronic records);
  • Locally digitized materials (institutional projects); and
  • Electronic Publications.

RLG has identified task force members from DLF partner and allied institutions and work is expected to begin in June.

Digital Library Services: Discovery and retreival services

Distributed Finding Aids

This collaborative project under the direction of John-Price Wilkin at the University of Michigan is exploring the means and costs of searching encoded finding aids that are distributed at different institutions. The final report was due in April. A no-cost extension was granted until June 30, 1999.

Reference Linking

In February, the DLF joined the National Information Standards Organization (NISO), the Society of Scholarly Publishers (SSP), and the National Federation of Indexing and Abstracting Services (NFAIS) in sponsoring a workshop on linkage from citations to the electronic journal literature. The workshop brought together publishers, librarians, abstracting and indexing services, repositories, vendors of information services, and end users, in order to build a common awareness of a broad range of needs and to improve understanding of the strengths and limitations of current approaches. In addition, the workshop sought to identify and stimulate actions needed to improve the facilities for linking citations and digital objects in the digital environment. The report of the workshop is available at http://www.niso.org/linkrpt.html.

As a result of the workshop, the sponsors created a small task group to explore the issues more fully and particularly to define the nature and scope of research, user consultation and other kinds of work necessary to foster the development of general systems of reference linking for marketplace testing. The report of the group was issued in May and is available at http://www.lib.uchicago.edu/Annex/pcaplan/reflink.html. The report outlines a general framework for the development of reference linking systems and formed the basis for a second invitational workshop held on June 9. Among the results of the most recent workshop was a action plan for additional research by computer scientists and a call for development, led by DLF, to solve the problem of selective resolution when there are multiple copies of a cited work. For more details about the second workshop, including the report when it is completed, see http://www.niso.org/linkge2.html.

Framework for institutional development and collaboration

Management forums

The Digital Library Federation's first semi-annual Forum on Digital Library Practices will be held in Washington D.C. the weekend of July 17-18 at the Doyle Hotel. Professor Gary Marchionini of the faculty of the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill's School of Information and Library Science will launch the one and one-half day forum with a plenary address entitled Expanding Library Services in the Digital Age: The Search for Equilibrium. Digital library practitioners from DLF institutions will hear presentations from other practitioners on the topics of authentication and authorization systems, digital repositories, finding aids, page image navigation systems, and naming systems. The structure of the event has been developed to allow for significant opportunities for discussion and exchange. Confirmation letters will be sent to presenters by mid-June. Confirmations and an updated agenda will be sent to attendees immediately thereafter.

Other activities

Publications. In June, the Digital Library Federation released its second publication, Preserving the Whole: A Two-track Approach to Rescuing Social Science Data and Metadata, by Ann Green, JoAnn Dionne, and Martin Dennis. CLIR and DLF have jointly commissioned David Bearman to write a paper about a migration-based solution to preserving digital information.

Site visits. Waters visited the University of Virginia as a prelude to receiving an application to join the Federation. The University of Virginia will probably wait until a new Director is appointed before joining. Graham visited the University of Chicago Library.

Outreach. Waters continued to participate in the United States Department of Agriculture Digital Publications Preservation Steering Committee. He participated in a NSF Workshop on Data Archival and Information Preservation, and in the Second International Virtual Libraries Conference at the New York Public Library, which was devoted largely to the long-term maintenance of digital materials. He sat on panels to review proposals submitted to the joint JISC/NSF International Digital Libraries Research Collaboration and to the Canada Foundation for Innovation. He also met with visiting archivists from Germany to discuss possible joint ventures. Graham and Waters both participated with other DLF members as advisors in a NSF Workshop to organize the creation of a digital library by states participating in the Experimental Program to Stimulate Competitive Research (EPSCoR).

Waters discussed the "The State of the Art in Digital Preservation" at the 8th Annual Federal Depository Library Conference and "Changing roles in archiving" at the Society for Scholarly Publishing seminar on Archiving Journal Literature. At the CNI Spring Task Force meeting, he made a presentation on issues governing the sustainability of digital libraries as organizations, while Graham led a session on the DLF certificate authorization prototype project. Waters also presented the results of the DLF Workshop on Social Science Data Archives at the 25th Annual Conference of the International Association of Social Science Information Service and Technology (IASSIST). All presentations are available at http://www.diglib.org/dlfpresent.htm.
