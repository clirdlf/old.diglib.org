Building the scholarly commons

 

Recommendations submitted for consideration by the DLF Steering Committee at its meeting of 22 September 2000.

 

D Greenstein

September 8 2000

 

1. Executive summary

 

In its charter, the DLF states its aim as bringing “together -- from across the nation and beyond -- digitized materials that will be made accessible to students, scholars, and citizens everywhere, and that document the building and dynamics of America's heritage and cultures”. Its progress (and that of the library community more generally) in providing the level of interoperability envisaged in this mission statement has been slowed by the limitations inherent in available solutions.

 

The DLF has been working on two fronts whose convergence may substantially advance our key interest in this area. Notably it has worked on a registry of DLF members’ online public access collections of digitally reformatted materials, and on a technical framework that promises maximum interoperability at minimum cost to data providers and data owners.

 

This paper introduces this work and requests members of the DLF to adopt the technical framework and expose for collection by prototype Internet portal services, the metadata available in online public access collections of digitally reformatted materials and in selected online finding aids for special and archived collections.

 

2. The DLF Registry

 

The DLF has developed a registry of its members’ online public access collections of digitally reformatted materials. The registry (an initial prototype of which is available through www.hti.umich.edu/cgi/d/dlfcoll/dlfcoll-idx) demonstrates the wealth of scholarly information available online from DLF members. When extended to include finding aids and other online indexes it will create an integrated glimpse of members’ collective holdings and consequently emerge as the online gateway that opens out onto the largest and most significant scholarly information resource available today.

 

The registry also demonstrates the very severe limitations that exist on gateway services that rely upon man-made records describing individual web-accessible collections. In particularly, such registries are difficult to maintain and provide a very poor, possibly even useless reference tool for scholars.

 

q       The maintenance issue is well documented. Records need to be created for new resources as they come online and, in an extensively distributed environment, appropriate reporting structures are difficult at best to maintain.  In addition, the collection descriptions and URLs for existing resources need constantly to be refreshed to reflect the constant state of change that so typifies Internet-based information.

q       As a reference tool, the registry does not penetrate deeply enough for scholars into the referenced databases. Imagine searching for works bearing on the life, times, and oeuvres of Charles Dickens in an online index that includes only a single record for each of the DLF members’ OPACs. Unless the collection level descriptions specifically include reference to Dickens-related work (unthinkable unless all collection strengths, possibly all works are also included in the descriptions), the interested scholar will walk away assuming the DLF libraries are dramatically under-represented in nineteenth-century English literature.

 

For these two reasons, the registry is envisaged as a only a single small step toward achieving the level of interoperability that the DLF aspires to.

 

3. The technical framework

The DLF has also supported work on a wholly new technical framework for accessing networked scholarly resources – one that combines the best of library and Internet techniques while overcoming the inherent limitations in hand-crafted registries, union catalogues, and distributed search services. The framework, developed originally by the Open Archives initiative (OAi – www.openarchives.org), adapts the harvesting technique employed by the Internet search engines. In it, there are data providers and service providers. A data provider agrees to support a simple harvesting protocol and to provide extracts of its metadata in a common, minimal-level format in response to harvest requests. It then records information about its collection in a shared registry. A service provider uses this registry to locate participating data providers, and uses the harvest protocol to collect metadata from them. The service provider is then able to build intellectually useful services, such as catalogs and portals to materials distributed across multiple sites. The framework has many virtues.

q       The effort to enable a data provider to support metadata harvesting is reasonable compared with that required to contribute to a union catalog or provide Z39.50 search access. After the repository is set up, its metadata can be harvested with relatively little intervention, providing the scalability that library approaches have lacked. The development of both inclusive and specialized search services is possible; in fact, the framework encourages the development of many search services competing in terms of functionality, audience, and business models, thereby enriching the entire research environment.

q       The framework could be used to expose the metadata in thousands of individual systems worldwide to central collection. For example, comprehensive collections of Americana or GIS data could be developed. This would make local repositories more generally known, and more generally useful, because researchers could search across previously unconnected materials. It would also illuminate the "dark matter" of the Internet-material that is hard or impossible to find if the user does not already know where it exists. The harvesting services that could be developed under the expanded Santa Fe framework are limited only by need and ingenuity.

q       The academic community could begin to ensure that services would be developed that express the values of that community-services that center on materials of research and educational interest, that provide honest and transparent ranking and retrieval, and that improve search quality by intelligently integrating metadata.

4. Developing prototype harvesting services

 

In order to evaluate the framework the DLF is working closely with the OAi and with The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation in order to initiate a number of highly innovative Internet services that promise to adopt it.

 

With regard to the OAi, the DLF has joined together with the Coalition for Networked Information (CNI) to surround the OAi with stability and organizational support sufficient to attract investment in its evaluation and adoption. In particular it:

q       has appointed a Steering Committee and will support its work in overseeing the OAi’s development and evaluation;

q       has supported work of a technical review group as required to prepare the framework for application in prototype harvesting services;

q       will provide some light-weight organizational support as required to maintain the OAi’s website and manage any change requests that are made in light of its early application experience.

 

 

With The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation, the DLF is brokering the development of a number of harvesting services that will help to evaluate technical, organizational, and legal aspects of the framework’s application in live service environments. Upon an invitation from the Foundation, the DLF distributed a call to its members seeking expressions of interest from those willing to play a role in the prototypes, either as data providers or as service providers. Members who responded to the call have been invited to a meeting convened in New York in October by The Foundation, from which, it is hoped, at least a number of funding applications may arise.

 

Although it is too early to say what prototype harvesting services will emerge, it is hoped that some activity may be initiated in harvesting services that present information along the following lines:

 

q       Harvesting services seeking to expose information content in any format (e.g. bibliographic, digital, sound, video, etc.) of interest in a particular discipline or area of study (e.g. Americana, southern American history)

q       Harvesting services seeking to expose information content of a particular format (e.g. visual images, EADs, GIS, statistical data, etc.)

q       Harvesting services seeking to expose information in any format and in many subject areas, tailoring that information content for use by a particular community, e.g. a campus or the members of a multi-campus consortium

q       Harvesting services that expose information content in any format and in many or all subjects using search-engine technologies and approximating an “academic Lycos”.

 

Of course any prototype harvesting service will require access to item-level metadata content exposed in conformance to the technical framework as recommended by the OAi.

 

DLF members are therefore called upon to adopt the Open Archives initiative’s technical framework and to expose for collection by prototype Internet harvesting services, the metadata available in online public access collections of digitally reformatted materials and in selected online finding aids for special and archived collections.
