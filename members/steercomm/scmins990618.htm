DLF Steering Committee

Minutes of meeting on June 18, 1999

Present: Scott Bennett (Yale), Harold Billings (University of Texas),Jerry Campbell (USC), Nancy Cline (Harvard), Nancy Eaton (Penn State), Rebecca Graham (DLF), Joan Gotwals (Emory), Bernie Hurley (for Gerald Lowell, UC Berkeley), Paula Kaufman (Tennessee), Michael Keller (Stanford), Wendy Lougee (for Bill Gosling, Michigan), Richard Lucier (CDL), Clifford Lynch (CNI), Deanna Marcum (CLIR), James Michalko (RLG), Paul Mosher (Penn), Donald Muccino (OCLC), Susan Nutter (NC State), Gloriana St. Clair (Carnegie Mellon), Tom Shaughnessy (Minnesota), Winston Tabb (LC), Sarah Thomas (Cornell), Suzanne Thorin (Indiana), Barbara Van Deventer (for Martin Runkle, Chicago), William Walker (NYPL), Donald Waters (DLF), Robert Wolven (for Elaine Sloan, (Columbia)

Unable to attend: L. Reynolds Cahoon (NARA), Karin Trainer (Princeton)

Paula Kaufman, chair, opened the meeting at 10:00 a.m. by welcoming the participants. She reminded the group that she will assume the position of university librarian at the University of Illinois in August, and since Illinois is not currently a member of DLF, she must resign as chair of the Federation. Sarah Thomas, last year's chair, has graciously agreed to fill the remainder of Ms. Kaufman's term until elections can be held.

The Steering Committee agreed to the meeting dates proposed for academic year 1999-2000: September 24, 1999; March 17, 2000; and June 16, 2000.

Don Waters reviewed highlights of the written report of DLF activities since the last Steering Committee meeting. Jim Michalko, in expanding upon the report of the task forces being established by the Research Libraries Group, said that there are not enough DLF institutions to populate the institutional records task force. Other institutions are volunteering representatives for that group. Task forces will begin their work in July.

Clifford Lynch noted that the small groups that worked on technical metadata elements for images in the recent workshop would soon distribute substantive technical analyses of metadata requirements.

Mr. Waters announced that there would be a written report on the reference-linking workshop. He observed that linking to resources of which there are multiple versions remains a thorny problem, and that the National Science Foundation has initiated international collaboration on reference linking research.

The DLF activities report prompted a discussion of how all projects fit into a broader framework. The members of the Steering Committee have diverse notions about how much structure is actually needed, but the group concluded that an annual review of DLF projects might provide a continuing synthesis that would be quite valuable.

Mr. Waters excused himself from the meeting while the Steering Committee discussed its aspirations for the new director of the Federation. The group praised Mr. Waters' wonderful combination of technical knowledge and leadership qualities. Everyone agreed that the combination is desirable in the next director as well. As one director commented, "Don has provided the glue among our organizations. That function continues to be more important than any other." Communication skills are also extremely important in the next director; DLF needs someone who can link to other administrative groups, who can connect with the many academic disciplines, and who can link all of the institutions within DLF. Mr. Lynch commented on the productive collaboration that CNI and DLF had developed, and all agreed that this is important to continue.

Directors suggested looking at the former Planning Committee, at individuals abroad who are doing work similar to DLF projects, and at the medical informatics community for candidates to fill the position. While all agree that the desirable educational background was not limited to any one field, one DLF participant urged that the next director be able to look at problems through a "librarian's lens."

Moving to a discussion of the communications program of the DLF, directors recommended that summaries of all activities be posted on the DLF Web site. They encouraged two types of published reports--one that focuses on broader issues that will appeal to administrators and one that goes more deeply into technical issues. Some directors thought DLF staff should survey the landscape and alert directors of forthcoming trends, but others thought that this would diffuse the DLF focus. Several directors advised DLF staff to issue more reports that are focused on the institutional accomplishments of member organizations.

All members of the Federation were encouraged to submit names of possible candidates to Deanna Marcum, chair of the search committee.

Lunch ended with a toast to Mr. Waters, wishing him every success in his new position at The Andrew W. Mellon Foundation and an expression of gratitude to Ms. Kaufman for her leadership of the Steering Committee.

In the afternoon session, David Millman, Columbia University, presented a report on the prototype for certificate-based authentication and authorization (see attachment 1), followed by a report on the academic image exchange project by Max Marmor, Yale University (see attachment 2).

The Steering Committee applauded the work done by these two groups, calling them the very model of federated work they had hoped for.

Questions asked about the certificate-based authentication and authorization work included the following:

• What are the encryption methods used?

• What amount of time is required for the user to go through the process?

• Is there an over-emphasis on what service providers want rather than what libraries want?

•How are privacy issues dealt with?

• Does this require libraries to issues certificates to every user?

• What are the technical requirements for implementing this system?

• Are administrative software systems producers being kept informed of this work?

• Are the other models that are under development in other countries being considered?

The directors encouraged Mr. Millman and the DLF staff to survey each of the DLF institutions to determine what each is doing in this area. They also urged that the work be made known to various licensing consortia.

The following questions were asked about the image exchange:

• Does it make more sense to think about distributed repositories after the prototype is completed?

• Can the scope be broadened to include more general research needs, not just curricular support?

• Should archaeology, architecture, and anthropology images eventually be added to the image exchange?

• Will the project address persistent identifier schemes as one of its components?

• How is this project being integrated into other DLF projects?

Mr. Marmor indicated that the College Art Association recently endorsed the project, and there are many enthusiastic volunteers now at work. Mr. Waters reported that DLF has thus far invested $25,000 in this project, but another $50,000 will be needed to complete the prototype. All directors indicated support for completing the prototype.

Written reports from three DLF partners had been distributed prior to the meeting. Directors had an opportunity to ask questions about the reports.

The meeting was adjourned at 3:00 p.m.
