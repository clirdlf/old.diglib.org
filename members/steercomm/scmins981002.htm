DLF Steering Committee

Minutes of meeting on October 2, 1998

Present: Scott Bennett (Yale), Liz Bishoff (OCLC, for Donald Muccino), Jerry Campbell (USC), Laura Campbell (LC, for Winston Tabb), Nancy Cline (Harvard), Rebecca Graham (DLF), Joan Gotwals (Emory), Paula Kaufman (Tennessee), Michael Keller (Stanford), Wendy Lougee (Michigan, for Bill Gosling), Richard Lucier (CDL), Deanna Marcum (CLIR), Charlene Mason (Minnesota, for Tom Shaughnessy), Paul Mosher (Penn), Susan Nutter (NC State), Sue Rosenblatt (UC Berkeley, for Penny Abell), Martin Runkle (Chicago), Gloriana St. Clair (Carnegie Mellon), Diana Shenk (Penn State, for Nancy Eaton), Elaine Sloan (Columbia), Sarah Thomas (Cornell), Suzanne Thorin (Indiana), Karin Trainer (Princeton), William Walker (NYPL), Donald Waters (DLF), Linda West (RLG, for James Michalko)

Unable to attend: L. Reynolds Cahoon (NARA)

Guests: Dr. Michael Kurtz, Scientist, Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics

Sarah Thomas, chair, welcomed participants and opened the meeting at 10:00 a.m.

Minutes of the last meeting were approved. Participants encouraged distributing an executive summary of the minutes on the Web.

Rebecca Graham, the new Research Associate, was warmly welcomed.

The nominating committee submitted its recommendation that Paula Kaufman be the next chair of the Steering Committee. Ms. Kaufman was elected by acclamation.

Review of DLF Activities. Don Waters noted that his written report was included in the pre-meeting packet. Questions from the group were answered. Most of the time allocated for the activity report was spent on weighing the relative interest in proposed activities. Several participants cited the interest of their institutions in distance education and urged Don to pursue this area vigorously by appointing a task force to outline possible next steps. Michael Keller pointed out that this area is also of great interest to the commercial sector, and he asked that the task force should make an effort to find out if DLF could take concerted action to make better use of its purchasing power. Nancy Cline encouraged forming a partnership with Educause in pursuing this subject. Jerry Campbell urged that the task force should try to consider the entire digital learning environment (both technology in the classroom and digital libraries).

On the topics of government documents and GIS, there was also a great deal of interest in further action. Mr. Keller noted the DLI work at Berkeley and Santa Barbara on GIS systems that may be useful for DLF purposes. Richard Lucier indicated that the California Digital Library has completed prelimionary evaluations of the usefulness of these two projects in a production environment.. He offered to make the evaluation results available to the DLF partners. Charlene Mason asked that a technology specialist and a government documents specialist be engaged to consider the next steps. Mr. Waters will ask the Steering Committee members by e-mail for suggestions of staff to work on a government documents project.

Mr. Waters described his site visit to Carnegie-Mellon University, and noted the interesting work that has been completed recently by John Ockerbloom on agent-based architecture for digital file conversion and migration. The Steering Committee was enthusiastic about pursuing Ockerbloom's idea more fully for DLF purposes. Some members wondered if we should not also consider the JSTOR model for managing electronic journals at the same time. Wendy Lougee conveyed the importance of a concurrent discussion about how we share middleware.

Ms. Rosenblatt volunteered that MOA III as it has been discussed by several DLF members is a project involving archiving strategies, particularly migration. Mr. Waters observed that events are already overtaking that aspect of the project. Library of Congress, Michigan, and others are already collaborating to migrate digitized materials from obsolete and inaccessible systems into more accessible formats.

Mr. Lucier observed that archiving is a core issue for DLF participants. It is this question that raises very serious doubts about digital libraries among faculty. Martin Runkle suggested that the phrase "long term maintenance" be substituted for digital archiving in order to attract administrators' attention. He reminded the group that long term maintenance of digital files is critically important to all institutions. He recommended that DLF conduct a financial analysis of a service we could offer collectively, possibly using the Center for Research Libraries model. Mr. Keller summarized the discussion by observing that the institutions represented in the DLF are perhaps uniquely positioned to bring the necessary resources to bear to solve the digital archiving problem. There was general agreement that DLF should take this issue as a matter of urgent priority and make a visible, public commitment to progress in various settings and ways.

With all of the possible activities being important, what are the core activities for the next year, the group asked. Mr. Waters was asked to present a plan for DLF-defined service by the next meeting of the Steering Committee. Scott Bennett suggested that all of our institutions are currently spending millions of dollars on data conversion activities, even though they it is not always clear how the work advances the mission of the libraries involved. DLF should seek to help tie the business plans for data conversion activities to the general business plan for the libraries.

When asked what he felt the other priorities are of DLF, Mr. Waters responded with four: archiving (or "long-term maintenance"), authorization, metadata, and discovery and retrieval services.

News from DLF Partners. Half of the DLF partners had an opportunity to describe current digital library initiatives that were underway in their institutions. Those who had submitted DLI 2 proposals to the National Science Foundation briefly described them.

Framework for Managing Digital Information. A paper by Rodney Stenlake describing a framework for libraries to license digital material that they own in their collections was discussed by the Steering Committee. The group concluded that the paper did not advance the collective thinking very much. Several possible approaches were offered, but the conclusion was it is too early to tackle this questions of licenses among member institutions.

Scholar-driven information systems. After a lunch break, the group reassembled to hear a presentation from Dr. Michael Kurtz, a scientist at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics. Dr. Kurtz described the center's efforts to create the Astrophysics Data System, beginning in 1990. He characterized the most important features of the data center as:

ADS is an international discipline-wide collaboration.

ADS personnel are experts in astronomy.

ADS was designed to work for end-users who are professional astronomers, not for the casually interested. Astronomers are the users of the service.

All ADS use is desktop use.

All astronomical research journals send their tables of contents to ADS. Seven of the eight most important journals provide online text in HTML. ADS has copyright permission from all of the journal publishers to scan the historical in-print literature into the database. In the future, the ADS plans to scan all journals to volume one. A grant from NEH is allowing ADS to work with the Harvard preservation staff to microfilm the volumes, and scanning will be done from the microfilm. Indexing will be done later as money is available. The ADS has been able to accomplish this largely because the scholarly societies in astrophysics own the materials and are willing to provide rights to include them in the database.

After discussing the implications of these scholar-driven information systems, the DLF Steering Committee adjourned. The next meeting is scheduled for March 19,1999.
