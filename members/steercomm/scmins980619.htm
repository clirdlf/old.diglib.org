DLF Steering Committee

Minutes of meeting on June 19, 1998

Present: Scott Bennett (Yale), Liz Bishoff (OCLC, for Donald Muccino), L. Reynolds Cahoon (NARA), Jerry Campbell (USC), Nancy Cline (Harvard), Nancy Eaton (Penn State), William Gosling (Michigan), Martin Halbert (Emory, for Joan Gotwals), Paula Kaufman (Tennessee), Richard Lucier (CDL), Deanna Marcum (CLIR), Assunta Pisani (Stanford, for Michael Keller), Patricia Renfro (Penn, for Paul Mosher), Sue Rosenblatt (UC Berkeley, for Peter Lyman), Martin Runkle (Chicago), Gloriana St. Clair (Carnegie Mellon), Elaine Sloan (Columbia), Winston Tabb (LC), Sarah Thomas (Cornell), Suzanne Thorin (Indiana), William Walker (NYPL), Donald Waters (DLF), Linda West (RLG, for James Michalko)

Unable to attend: Susan Nutter (NC State), Tom Shaughnessy (Minnesota), Karin Trainer (Princeton)

Guests: Ann Okerson (Yale)

Sarah Thomas, chair, convened the meeting at 10:00 a.m. Participants introduced themselves. Minutes of the last meeting were approved as written, and dates for upcoming meetings were approved (October 2, 1998; March 19, 1999; June 18, 1999).

Thomas welcomed the new partners - California Digital Library, University of Chicago, North Carolina State, and Carnegie Mellon - in the Federation.

Review of DLF activities. Don Waters highlighted Federation activities since the last meeting. He especially noted the following:

  • The first draft of the white paper on the Making of America project, which is receiving good feedback from the computer science community;
  • The CIC authorization project, which is moving along to a first phase deadline early in September;
  • The draft report, composed by Caroline Arms, on the access management workshop held in April. The report makes an important distinction between access and use; where management of access cannot reliably predict and may inappropriately constrain uses of information, especially in teaching and research contexts;
  • The development of the workshop scheduled for June 30 on editorial practices of electronic text centers.

Waters indicated that he had submitted a proposal on behalf of the DLF to the Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS) for funding for the Social Science Databases project and the Making of America, Part III. He also submitted a letter of intent to submit a proposal in the Digital Library Initiative grant program to study naming of works in multiple repositories. He has postponed submitting the proposal until the next round of grants.

In discussing organizational issues, Waters announced that the National Archives wishes to be an ally, not a partner, in the Federation. Projects of interest to the archival community will be sought in the coming months in which to involve NARA.

Waters' highest immediate priority is to fill the research associate position. Several candidates have been interviewed, but the search has not been successful.

Waters asked for the participants' views on the external review panel. He reminded the group that the panel is to be made up of individuals from outside the Federation with an interest and expertise to assess the work of the DLF once a year. The panel will review plans and help with "out-of-the-box" thinking. More than a dozen names were suggested, and it was decided that Waters will send an e-mail message to the partners reminding them of the need to think about faculty or others not usually listed for this assignment. Waters will then name a subcommittee of three or four Steering Committee members to make the final determination of the panel's composition.

Waters announced that the DLF Web site is now part of CLIR's website. Older material that has been maintained by the Library of Congress on its Web site will be archived. He also noted that a brochure to be used in fund-raising for DLF is being developed.

Scott Bennett urged that DLF begin to pursue non-library members. He encouraged Waters to make preliminary inquiries about the level of interest among the publishing and technology communities, in particular.

Bill Gosling encouraged Waters to communicate more detailed information to levels of staff beyond the director level.

Moving to a discussion of work to be undertaken, Waters cited the need to draw together the work that has been done by the architecture committee and to make it more widely available. He is thinking about commissioning a paper on digital library architecture, and asked the group for their thoughts about this. There was general agreement that such a paper would be helpful.

Bill Walker described a meeting recently held in Paris that was co-sponsored by the Bibliotheque nationale de France and the New York Public Library to discuss digital library initiatives. It was suggested that the Europeans consider forming a "European Digital Library Federation" as a body to continue discussions with the American counterpart, the Digital Library Federation. In discussing how to proceed, Gosling suggested that DLF serve an information clearinghouse function, so that all will be better informed about what is happening in other parts of the world. Richard Lucier cautioned that our need for international involvement is not limited to Europe. We need to be looking to the Pacific Rim and south of the border, as well. Bennett agreed that staying alert to opportunities for international collaboration is important, but cautioned that discussion of such opportunities should not be allowed to impede progress on the ambitious program of projects that the DLF has set for itself.

In summarizing the prospective list of projects, Waters emphasized that projects can move forward only where there is leadership from member institutions. To make progress in finding the appropriate relationship with the developing cottage industries, Deanna Marcum suggested bringing one or two scholars engaged in solitary digital projects to the next meeting of the Steering Committee to discuss what would be involved in connecting their projects to institutionalized digital libraries. Liz Bishoff urged that users of the cottage industry products be brought into the discussion as well.

Intellectual property policies. Turning to the campus initiatives related to copyright, there were brief presentations from Stanford, the University of California, Yale, Columbia, the University of Southern California, and Indiana University.

The group adjourned for lunch, and reconvened to discuss licensing issues.

Models for inter-institutional licensing. Waters referred to the position paper he prepared for the meeting in which he suggests that the Steering Committee should consider licensing as a strategic tool for digital libraries in making materials available to their readers. Licensing those materials would enable libraries to clearly articulate the value of their digital materials, and to define the conditions for high quality services to readers. Libraries have learned in their licensing arrangements with publishers that such features are essential for a robust development of digital materials. By developing models for inter-institutional licensing arrangements, the DLF might apply what institutions have learned in their relationships with publishers. Such models could serve as a foundation for enhancing the development of digital libraries and positioning them strategically to deal with changes in scholarly communication prompted by changes in institutional copyright policies.

Ann Okerson was to have led the Steering Committee in a discussion of inter-institutional licensing, but the schedule had to be modified because her plane was delayed. Waters read from some of Okerson's preliminary notes for her presentation and described five potential licensing environments for DLF to consider:

  1. No agreement or license is needed; material is freely available and widely distributed
  2. Unfettered distribution within membership; no conditions for use within DLF, but conditions apply for those not in the Federation
  3. Trading partners within membership; an interlibrary loan model applies with tiered arrangements within DLF and higher costs and additional conditions outside the Federation
  4. Costs recovered from and conditions imposed on all readers, regardless of the institution
  5. Costs recovered from libraries and conditions imposed on readers via their association with the library; higher costs and additional conditions would apply for readers not associated with a library

Again reading from Okerson's preliminary notes, Waters cited factors that would lead to freer, unrestricted access, such as the small, intimate nature of the group and shared values about disseminating information. Factors that would lead to more controlled access include the need for income to pay for the support of the site; the need for credit or attribution, and a conviction that business-like arrangements lead to cleaner relationships over time.

Nancy Cline wondered if the group could envision an umbrella model for licensing that incorporates the many arrangements already in effect in the member libraries. Bennett offered that Okerson's work suggests that no single all-encompassing license can be written. Moreover, he pointed out that she has expressed deep puzzlement about the issue of fair use, observing that libraries are now writing licenses that are more favorable than the current fair use provisions available under the copyright law. Bennett speculated that our old allegiance to fair use might not serve us well in the digital world. He suggested that we are witnessing a failure of the copyright regime to protect our interests and values, and that by engaging in a contract law environment that protects, even enhances, our traditional values.

Campbell noted the universal feeling that the issues surrounding digital ownership are complex, but essential to solve. DLF institutions must take up the challenge of managing the intellectual property they own. He cited the USC initiatives to develop or secure software that is sophisticated enough to recognize various levels of ownership as well as to recognize the rights of different owners. Working with the individual who created the credit card billing system for oil companies, USC has completed functional specification for this kind of system.

Asunta Pisani urged the group not to abandon the struggle over fair use. She wants to counter the publishers' assumption that libraries are fighting this as an economic question.

The large group then divided into three smaller groups to debate the resolution: That libraries, archives, and special collections should adopt, as a matter of policy, the use of licenses to make their digital materials available across inter-institutional boundaries.

Okerson arrived as the Steering Committee reconvened from the small group discussions. She described the differences between copyright and licenses as legal vehicles for providing access to materials that libraries own. She described the current practices of managing electronic resources and emphasized that copyright does not give any ownership rights to public domain materials. However, an institution can assert some authority over such materials through the use of licensing and this a critical point for DLF institutions that are digitizing public domain information. Okerson also described the current trends in licensing and noting the movement toward simplification through the development of licensing templates and frameworks. After reviewing the varying ways that DLF institutions are serving as creators and providers of digital resources, she observed that they are acting more or less like publishers. She recommended the use of copyright when it is part of the institutional mission and when the institution wants or needs no controls in the future. She recommended the use of licenses when income is desired or required, a diverse, layered marketplace exists, expectations and commitments need to be clear between the customer and provider, and/or when relationships between creator and customer need to be created and sustained over time.

Waters said that he would circulate the notes of the small group discussions and, in consultation with members of the Steering Committee, decide how best to move the discussions on licensing forward.

With thanks to all for their participation, and to Okerson for her persistence in reaching D.C. to attend, Thomas adjourned the meeting at 3:00 p.m.

<End of minutes, DLF Steering Committee meeting, 6/19/98>
