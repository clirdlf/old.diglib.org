DLF Steering Committee

Minutes of meeting on March 20, 1998

Present: S. Bennett (Yale), R. Cahoon (NARA), J. Campbell (USC), N. Cline (Harvard), B. Gosling (Michigan), J. Gotwals (Emory), P. Kaufman (Tennessee), M. Keller (Stanford), C. Mason (Minnesota, for T. Shaughnessy), S. Rosenblatt (UC Berkeley, for P. Lyman), E. Sloan (Columbia), S. Thomas (Cornell), S. Thorin (Indiana), K. Trainer (Princeton), W. Tabb (LC), W. Walker (NYPL), D. Waters (DLF), L. West (RLG, for J. Michalko)

Absent: N. Eaton (Penn State), D. Marcum (CLIR), P. Mosher (U Pennsylvania), D. Muccino (OCLC)

Guests: Michael Lesk (NSF) and George Farr (NEH) at lunch

Waters brought the meeting of the DLF Steering Committee to order at 10:10 a.m. on March 20, 1998. He welcomed those present and thanked everyone for their graciousness in hosting him for the site visits he made during the last five months. He also thanked them for their energetic and thoughtful participation in the three subcommittees on Program, Organization, and Budget. Their participation made the program, organization, and budget work truly a joint effort and the results to be reviewed in this meeting a joint product.

In the review of the agenda, Waters asked permission to take one item out of the proposed order, namely the question of the Steering Committee chair.

Steering Committee chair. Waters noted that as Director of the DLF he was an ex officio member of the Steering Committee, subject to the oversight of the Committee, and so should not serve as Committee chair. He said that he had convened a nominating committee consisting of Cline, Gosling, and Sloan to nominate a chair. They nominated Thomas, and she had agreed just prior to the meeting to serve a one-year term. Waters formally turned the chair over to Thomas, but said that he would still help conduct the meeting because she had no time to prepare.

Program direction. The Committee reviewed the definition, the institutional goals, the program priorities, and the projects and plans, as described in the program document. During the ensuing discussion, the following issues were among those raised:

  • It is vital for the DLF to represent the scholarly community and the interests of fair use against the commercial publishing arena. However, under the umbrella of scholarly interests, the interests of scholars as authors sometimes conflict with the interests of scholars as teachers.
  • The institutional goals that DLF seeks to support should not be viewed as fixed in time. The work of the DLF, for example, should be regarded as a discovery process in which DLF might identify from its work new goals that institutions will incorporate in their priorities.
  • There is not a clear mapping of the DLF priorities and its projects and plans to the institutional goals DLF aims to support. The revision of the program document should seek to clarify the relationships among these three components.
  • The proposed Michigan/Harvard project posits a complicated and expensive retrieval system and so may be misdirecting DLF resources. Waters said that the authors themselves recognized problems in the proposal and were revising it. Further, Waters said he will follow the practice that CLIR uses to subject unsolicited proposals like this one to a blind peer review before approving them for funding.
  • The DLF plans for work on authorization may lead to overly complicated designs, when "adequate" technical solutions would instead prove sufficient. Mason and Waters described the plans for the CIC authorization project, which call for a phased approach and detailed evaluations after each phase to ensure that the technical design matches the problems that the project is trying to solve.
  • The description of DLF program work to provide organizational support for library directors needs to be clarified. It should emphasize that there are a number of program topics to which the library directors are in the best position to contribute.

Subject to these concerns and qualifications, which will be addressed in a revised program statement, the Steering Committee endorsed the proposed program agenda.

DLI-II. Michael Lesk (NSF) and George Farr (NEH) joined the Committee at noon to discuss the recently announced Phase II of the Digital Library Initiative grant program. Over lunch, Lesk described the focal areas of the recently announced DLI-II program and contrasted it with the Knowledge and Distributed Intelligence program. He also mentioned work underway to link digital library research in the U.S. to related research abroad in an international program that NSF is developing.

Lesk emphasized that, compared to DLI-I, the DLI-II program specifically includes user-oriented research and applied research themes. Moreover, the criteria for evaluating proposed projects is designed to help select projects that hold the greatest promise for sustainability. He said that these features were all intended to ensure that the program reached a broad audience of researchers, not just computer scientists. He said that NSF is particularly interested in attracting the research interests and direct involvement of libraries such as those represented in the DLF. Farr added that these features had attracted the interest of NEH as a co-sponsor of DLI-II, and he too hoped that DLF institutions would participate in the program by generating compelling proposals.

After Lesk and Farr left, Waters asked for advice on what role the DLF should aim to play in the DLI-II program. The advice of the Committee was that DLF should aim to submit a proposal, provided that it complements and supports, but does not directly conflict with work that partner institutions are individually proposing.

Organizational Issues. The Committee discussed the principles of organization, the categories of participation, the internal organization and the by-law proposals regarding payment of DLF fees, the disbursement of DLF funds for travel, and the principles for disbursement of the capital fund. During the ensuing discussion, members of the Steering Committee made the following points:

  • The criterion for partnership - that prospective partners must demonstrate that they are operating digital libraries - should explicitly refer to the working definition of digital library that DLF has adopted.
  • Perhaps some explanation should be offered in the document about why DLF regards RLG and OCLC as formal allies.
  • A qualification should be added that the Steering Committee "normally" meets three times a year, to allow for the possibility of both more and less meetings depending on the circumstances.
  • The DLF should be sure to draw from the university community as a high priority when forming the Review Panel.
  • The by-law on travel for the Technical Architecture Committee should be reworded so that it is clear that systems staff members are not the only ones in libraries facing stiff competition for their time and attention.
  • The by-law on travel for staff in DLF institutions should be rephrased to emphasize that the constraint against funding staff travel is the availability of funds within DLF, not the discretion of the DLF director.
  • The Director should be subject to a limit on the amount he can dispense without additional approvals.

Subject to these qualifications, which will be addressed in a revised document, the Steering Committee approved the proposed statement of organization and by-laws.

Non-payment of fees. In light of the by-laws just approved regarding payment of DLF fees, the Committee asked Waters to identify any partners who had not paid their fees. He should address the matter with them and report back at the next meeting.

New Partners. After reviewing the application of the California Digital Library (CDL) to become a strategic partner within the DLF, the Steering Committee agreed to welcome the CDL as a new partner. The Steering Committee further agreed to streamline the process for considering prospective partners. It asked the Director to ensure that prospective partners meet the criteria for partnership and describe how they meet the criteria in writing. When the Director is satisfied with an application, he should refer it to a subcommittee of the Steering Committee for review. If the subcommittee is satisfied then the Director may accept the application without referring it to the full Steering Committee.

Some members expressed concern that the Steering Committee is reaching a size in which meetings of a committee-of-the-whole are becoming unmanageable. They suggested that the DLF should place an upper limit on the number of new partners it will accept. While recognizing these concerns, the Committee agreed to postpone further discussion of limits on the number of partners until a later time, when clearer evidence is in hand about actual interest in DLF.

Budget. The Steering Committee reviewed and approved the revised operating budget for FY97/98. It also reviewed and approved the proposed operating budget for FY98/99. The Committee approved a motion to continue to charge $19,000 as the annual operating fee for the next fiscal year.

Before the Committee adjourned, members asked if the program agenda and statement of organization could be made available for distribution within DLF institutions when the documents are revised. Waters said that he had begun to relocate the DLF web site from the LC web to CLIR's web site. The program agenda and the statement of organization will both be included on the web site. Moreover, a revision of the documents will also be available in paper form as soon as possible. In addition, CLIR is assisting DLF in the preparation of a brochure that will be designed for DLF partners to distribute to foundations and other funding agencies.

The meeting adjourned at 2:50 p.m.

<End of minutes, DLF Steering Committee meeting, 3/20/98>
