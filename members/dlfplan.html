Executive summary

The draft strategy and business plan derives from an intensive review of key challenges confronted by DLF members in developing their digital libraries. The review acted in part as an informal evaluation of the DLF and that perspective has been brought to bear on the strategy's construction.

This executive summary highlights key proposals and includes recommendations arising in the course of the strategy's preliminary review. It reflects the numerous comments received by email, and the more focused consideration of the draft strategy given by a small group convened on 8-9 March 2000 in Ann Arbor.

The review clearly demonstrated the accomplishments already achieved by the DLF through members' collaboration e.g. in leading edge digital library research and development, in enhancing understanding of good practices, and in building community amongst digital library practitioners. It also revealed an interest in:

  • activity beyond the architectural and technical areas where DLF has focused;

  • greater emphasis on evaluating, documenting, and disseminating the results of work conducted within the DLF;

  • greater inclusiveness within work conducted within the DLF;

  • greater information sharing amongst member institutions about their digital library practices, strategies, organizations, etc.

  • greater emphasis on community building amongst digital library practitioners;

  • continued emphasis on attracting funding as essential to digital library research and development.

The following nine key proposals are offered for members' consideration.

  1. The DLF should have the following core aims (section 1.2):

    1. Promoting information sharing amongst members to raise awareness about digital library tools, methods, trends, and strategies, and to inform and help validate local digital library activities, decisions, and investments.

    2. Sharing digital library practices and experiences in a range of areas with a view to evolving understanding of good practice.

    3. Stimulating research and development work in areas that bear directly on the development, maintenance and use of digital libraries and are not being developed (or fully developed) by existing organizations.

    4. Attracting external funding to support essential digital library research and development.

    5. Responding quickly to digital library challenges as they arise.

    6. Working closely with proximate professional and other associations.

    7. Investing in and helping to build professional communities appropriate to the development of digital libraries and their staff.
  2. The DLF should continue working on technical and architectural issues (sections 2.1 and 2.3) while investigating other digital library challenges not fully engaged by existing organizations. Such challenges include:

    1. Collections strategies (section 2.2.) - that is content blind or content neutral issues surrounding the creation, development, and maintenance of digital collections (e.g. economic models, rights issues).

    2. Human factors (section 2.4) that bear on our understanding and development of digital library architectures, standards, tools, and collections.

    3. Digital preservation (section 2.5) particularly in the identification of good preservation practices and organizational and economic models.

    4. Strategic, policy, organizational, and economic issues (section 2.6) associated with the library's changing roles and institutional relationships.

    5. Research, development, and other activities that emerge outside of existing program areas or that cross those program areas (section 2.7).

  3. In addressing itself to these challenges, priority should be given to the following.

    1. Reconvening the "architecture committee" to advise the DLF in work on digital library standards, architectures and tools (work is underway or in preparation on structural and administrative and its use integrating digital library holdings; text and image metadata; mechanisms for integrating access to distributed, heterogeneous item-level digital library holdings; digital certificates; reference linking; persistent identifiers; "open source" software development and exchange; integrating access to pre-print services and collections).

    2. Establishing other expert advisory groups to investigate the viability of work in collections strategies (section 2.2), human factors (section 2.4), and strategic and policy areas (section 2.6).

    3. Convening selected task forces for example, to investigate models for developing, integrating access to, and preserving distributed social science and government data collections; to extend collaborative work conducted by representatives of DLF members' text centers; and to review and elaborate collection strategies and organizational and business models as being developed by the Academic Image Cooperative (under section 2.8).
  4. The DLF's organization should reflect is aims of agility; conducting, reviewing, and widely disseminating results of collaborative research and development work in key areas; information sharing and community building amongst the membership (section 3.1.). It should comprise a small Secretariat with catalytic, facilitating, and communications functions. It should also comprise a small Executive Committee responsible for the DLF's policies, programs, funding and evaluation, and for monitoring and advising the work of the Secretariat. The DLF should also include a number of working groups focusing in key areas so long as they remain viable and valuable to the membership and responsible for documenting, reviewing, and disseminating work in those areas for the benefit of the membership. Such groups may include:
    1. standing expert groups (called Program Advisory Groups) modeled after the architecture committee and taking responsibility for identifying and developing viable research and information sharing agendas and for building professional communities capable of addressing digital library challenges as they arise in key areas (e.g. collection strategies, human factors, etc);
    2. Task Forces convened on an ad hoc, short-term basis whether by Program Advisory Groups or by other means and responsible for focused research, development and other practical activities.
  5. The DLF should host a regular Forum (section 3.1.) to serve as meeting places and market places for the Executive Committee, the Program Advisory Groups, the Task Forces, and the broader professional community represented in the membership.

  6. The DLF should develop and maintain strategic alliances with library and other organizations that have a stake in or contribution to make to digital library developments (section 3.1.).

  7. The DLF should develop a communications infrastructure capable of facilitating, assessing, and disseminating outputs that result from its members' collaborative work, and of encouraging information exchange and community building across the membership (section 3.2.). At a minimum, the communications infrastructure should comprise mailing lists open to DLF members; a web-based publication series; regularly published web-based newsletter comprising brief progress reports on DLF initiatives and reports from members about any new grants, projects, services, collections, etc., and about any key challenges they are addressing locally.
  8. DLF funds should be distributed to facilitate collaborative efforts, information sharing, and community building amongst DLF members (Section 3.3). They should maintain a small secretariat and the DLF Forum, and help support expert (Program Advisory) groups. They should also assist Task Forces in their planning phases. All DLF initiatives should be encouraged and supported in their efforts to sustain themselves through participants' co-investment and with external funding, and the DLF should consider increasing funds available to the program as a whole.

  9. The DLF should move forward in three phases (Section 4) as follows:

    1. A brief and aggressive re-organizational phase (to June 2000) to establish essential organizational and communications mechanisms; to convene expert advisory groups to identify viable research, development, and information sharing agenda in key program areas (e.g. architectures, standards, and tools; collection strategies; human factors); and to convene task forces in selected areas.
    2. A longer developmental phase (to June 2001) during which time emphasis is shifted to the research, development, information sharing, and community building work of the expert groups, task forces, and Forums.
    3. An evaluative phase (to June 2002) extending the applied efforts referenced in 9.2. above and including an independent formal evaluation of the DLF that may inform any discussion about its future.

1. Introduction

The draft strategy and business plan is presented in four sections. Section 1 presents the definition of a digital library that emerged from the review. It identifies where members feel they are or should be active in order to develop effective, affordable, and high-quality digital libraries. It also presents aims for the DLF that represent consensus about where the organization should focus its efforts. Section 2 clusters into program areas convergent opinions about where the DLF should be active or at least interested if it is to achieve its members' aims and support their digital library ambitions. Section 3 reflects discussions about how the DLF should organize and support its efforts and includes a section on the communications infrastructure that the DLF must develop to advance its program. Section 4 makes a series of recommendations about next steps that DLF members might take to proceed with any of the program areas that are outlined in the document.

Members should consider this document as a proposal; strategic discussion about the following points is crucial:

  • the proposed program areas and the priorities that might be assigned to them;

  • the structure and operational mechanisms that will allow the membership to make some progress in any combination of program areas;

  • the costs of collaboration and how they may be met;

  • steps that members might take by to carry out program priorities that are agreed upon.

1.1. Some definitions

The digital library extends the breadth and scale of scholarly and cultural evidence and supports innovative research and life-long learning. To do this, it mediates between diverse and distributed information resources on the one hand and a changing range of user communities on the other. In this capacity, it establishes "a digital library service environment" - that is, a networked, online information space in which users can discover, locate, acquire access to and, increasingly, use information. Although access paths will vary depending upon the resource in question, the digital library service environment makes no distinctions among information formats. Books, journals, paper-based archives, video, film, and sound recordings are as visible in the digital library service environment as are online catalogs, finding aids, abstract and indexing services, e-journal and e-print services, digitized collections, geographic information systems, Internet resources, and other "electronic" holdings.

In constructing a digital library service environment, the library becomes responsible for configuring access to a world of information of which it owns or manages only a part. Accordingly, the digital library is known less for the extent and nature of the collections it owns than for the networked information space it defines through a range of online services. In the world of commercial publishing, aggregators compete on the basis of the value-added services that they layer on top of overlapping electronic collections. Similarly, digital libraries establish their distinctive identities, serve their user communities, emphasize their owned collections, and promote their unique institutional objectives by the way in which they disclose, provide access to, and support the use of their increasingly virtual collections.

The digital library service environment is not simply about access to and use of information. It also supports the full range of administrative, business and curatorial functions required by the library to manage, administer, monitor engagement with, and ensure fair use of its collections whether in digital or non-digital formats, whether located locally or off site. The digital library service environment integrates (and interfaces with) information repositories that are characterized by open-access shelving, high-density book stores and availability via inter-library loan, and include data services and digital archival repositories. It manages information about collections and items within collections often throughout their entire life cycle. It incorporates patron, lending, and other databases, and integrates appropriate procedures for user registration, authentication, authorization, and fee-transaction processing. The digital library service environment may also evolve into a networked learning space, providing access to and a curatorial home for distance and life-long learning materials. The digital library service environment is, in sum, an electronic information space that supports very different views and very different uses of the library. It is designed for the library's patrons as well as for its professional staff and with an eye on the needs and capacities of those who supply it with information content and systems. It is built in the full knowledge that information technologies will continue to change rapidly as will our understanding of how they may be used effectively to support education and cultural engagement. Finally it evolves as the library's single most important core service and as such is developed with a view to its financial and organizational sustainability.

1.2. The Digital Library Federation

The Digital Library Federation (DLF) is a consortium of research libraries that are transforming themselves and their historic educational and cultural roles through their exploitation of networked and digital technologies. Committed to pioneering innovation, they seek through their confederation to lever their collective influence, reputations, digital library investments, and research and development capacities To achieve the following:
  • lower the costs of developing digital library service environments;

  • gain maximum value for every dollar spent in digital library research and development;

  • achieve a greater degree of predictability and consistency in an information landscape where technologies and our understanding of appropriate organizational, economic, and legal structures, and of human behavior are all highly fluid; and

  • create a space in which a dynamic community of digital library practitioners may develop and flourish in the interests of promoting and advancing their shared profession.

The DLF is an organizational expression of its members' shared interests, their collective will, and their collaborative impulses. Members perceive the following roles for the Federation's secretariat:

  • to translate members' digital library aims into a shared vision and a program capable of realizing it, maintaining both in light of the members' changing needs and their changing perceptions of opportunities;

  • within the context of this program, to facilitate meaningful collaboration amongst the DLF members and between DLF members and other organizations;

  • to maximize the impacts of collaborative initiatives while lowering their entry and overhead costs;

  • to establish and maintain strategic alliances with common purpose library and other organizations in order to achieve economies of scale and to maximize impacts of complementary efforts;

  • to promote the work of the DLF and its members to the wider community thereby raising the organization's profile and extending the influence of its members' work; and/LI>

  • to establish liaison with strategic and funding bodies and to seek support from them for the DLF's program and its associated dissemination activities.

2. Program areas

This section reflects the clustering of opinion that emerged from the review about the areas in which the DLF should be active. Program areas are defined in relation to key challenges as commonly perceived across the membership. Strategies are proposed that might help the membership to confront those challenges collaboratively. Omitted from the document is any detailed discussion of the task-level work that might take place within program areas or the priorities that might be assigned to such work. As recommended in Section 3.1., those discussions, in order to meaningful and actionable agreement, need to take place among and be owned by expert Program Advisory Groups derived directly from the membership. Convening that discussion in Advisory Groups established for that purpose is therefore recommended in Section 4 as an immediate next step for the DLF.

2.1. Achieving greater consistency, predictability, and interoperability within digital library service environments

The challenge: In developing a digital library service environment, the library seeks to enable meaningful navigation through and exploitation of distributed and heterogeneous information resources that are stored and managed in different formats. Users require this level of integration. Interested in gaining access to relevant information quickly and efficiently, regardless of format and location, they are not contented with networked environments that require, for example, that queries be launched repeatedly at different collections and indices, or the incorporation of numerous authentication barriers. Collection development and maintenance, and business incentives too, also compel a more integrating approach. At present, new information resources and the managerial and user service functions that are associated with them are bolted onto digital library service environments in an ad hoc, often proprietary fashion. The approach does not scale cost effectively across the range of information resources and services that need to be incorporated into and supported by the digital library service environment; it has debilitating impacts on the environment's functionality, usefulness, administration and maintenance, and it creates tangible limitations on collections development and use.

The strategy: Seek greater consistency and predictability across the digital library service environment and interoperability among the information content and information services that are deployed within it by identifying and articulating minimum-level preferences for content and services, and levering the membership's collective influence to promote their widest possible adoption. The following may be conducted within this program area:

  1. Identifying and documenting minimum level preferences for the data and metadata formats of those content classes typically included in digital library service environments (e.g. online public access catalogues, finding aids, and other collection indices; arts and abstracting databases, electronic journals, electronic books, image and text databases, spatially referenced data, and statistical data).

  3. Identifying and documenting minimum level functional requirements for systems and services and an integrating architecture that identifies the inter-relationships and inter-dependencies of those systems and services, and includes references to appropriate and preferred standards and protocols.[1]

  5. Selecting content and system requirements and preferences with a view to their deployment in an integrating digital library service environment, their sustainability through changing technical regimes, their functionality and use, their extensibility, and the costs associated with their deployment, maintenance, and modification.

  7. Organizationally endorsing and promoting more widespread adoption of minimum level content and system preferences to:
    • focus, integrate, and minimize the costs of developing local digital library service environments;
    • influence and inform content and systems suppliers and developers thereby minimizing their impacts on the development and maintenance of local services.

  9. Developing and disseminating implementation guidelines, decision-making, and other tools to inform, encourage, and supply compelling incentives for the widest possible adoption and deployment of such preferences, recognizing that different communities may require different iterations of these tools.

2.2. Collections development

The challenge: A digital library provides access to both digital and non-digital objects. The library's "collections" are vastly extended to include third-party objects, information sources, and information services over which the library has little or no curatorial control.[2] The library's collection development functions are similarly extended. While maintaining its historic curatorial role with regard to analog and paper-based holdings, it takes on additional functions more closely associated with the digital repository or data service and the Internet portal.

The strategy: Develop, evaluate, and apply collection development strategies that reflect the fundamental changes in the nature, extent, maintenance, and use of library collections. Ensure that these strategies support the library as it seeks systematically, cost-effectively, and with minimum redundancy to work in integrated service environments to assemble, coherently present, and actively and programmatically manage deeply heterogeneous and extensively distributed scholarly and cultural information resources. The following tasks may be conducted within this program area:

  1. Developing and promoting minimum level content and service preferences (see Section 2.1. above) that take account of the need to integrate access to and management of heterogeneous information content drawn from:
    • locally managed paper-based and analog collections and the inter-library lending services that provide access to such collections managed by third parties;
    • digital information supplied commercially by third parties and which may be managed either locally or off site;
    • digital information available non-commercially from third parties and which may be managed locally or off site; and
    • digital objects created locally whether they are born digital or created as surrogates for locally managed paper-based and analog objects.

  3. Developing guidelines and tools for decision making that help ensure that all collection development decisions (that is decisions taken with regard to all of the information sources enumerated above) are informed with a view of the preferences referenced above and thus of the short-, medium-, and long-term costs associated with their inclusion by whatever means into a digital library service environment.

  5. Developing a better understanding of users' information requirements and their behaviors in digital library service environments (including methodologies appropriate to assessing and anticipating those requirements and behaviors) and informing all collection development decisions in light of that understanding (see Section 2.4. below).

  7. Extending the breadth and scale of collections by penetrating and mobilizing communities that produce non-commercial electronic information with educational and cultural value. By developing capacity as portal and repository services, and by offering tools and other incentives to data producers so that they turn to the library for access to those services, DLF members may lever their reputation and authority to significantly enhance their "collections". They stand in particular to benefit from:
    • Electronic indices and digital surrogates being created by regional libraries and other cultural organizations. Typically these organizations will wish to achieve a greater level of access to their online holdings than they can attract. Many will also recognize the importance of preserving these digital assets but will not have the capacity to mount appropriate or effective digital preservation programs.
    • Primary research and other "unpublished" data produced by academic communities and professional societies. Here, too, there is a growing recognition of the direct benefits that accrue from disclosing such data through more comprehensive, high-quality online portal services and securing them through changing technical regimes.
    • Educational and cultural data resources created with grant funding. Increasingly, funding agencies are becoming aware that the value of their investments in digital information are only fully realized if such data are assembled into professionally managed collections, made accessible in the context of related information, and maintained over the longer term.
    • Data produced by government agencies and commercial organizations and possessing substantial scholarly and cultural but [time] limited commercial value.

  9. Where commercially available data are concerned, initiating formal dialogue with their suppliers on a range of licensing, rights, business, and fair use issues that keep both libraries and data suppliers from taking best advantage of the opportunities afforded them in a digital environment. Here, the DLF might look for a model to the UK at the co-operative undertakings of the Higher Education Funding Councils' Joint Information Systems Committee and the Publishers' Association (PA). [3] Through a series of working parties, the JISC/PA has made progress in a number of important areas affecting the scope and latitude available to the library in how it manages, presents, encourages use of, and in selected cases re-purposes commercially supplied data.

  11. Placing the production of digital surrogates on a more programmatic and sustainable footing, such as by developing and disseminating guidelines that inform local digitization efforts with a view to:
    • their likely use (by whom and for what purpose);
    • the costs involved in their integration into local digital library service environments;
    • their potential for re-purposing so that they may support multiple uses and user communities, and the costs involved in that re-purposing; and
    • the costs associated in maintaining long-term access.

  13. Exploring and assessing the costs and benefits associated with various business models that promise to sustain digitization efforts and their resulting online services beyond their initial funding regimes by:
    • using techniques derived from market research to assess likely demand for and likely use of digital collections derived from library holdings;
    • undertaking realistic and formal cost-benefit assessment of revenue generating strategies other than those based on access-based fee structures (e.g. corporate sponsorship of digital collections, online advertising and through-sales, wholesale licensing of collections to aggregators and other third parties, or strategic alliances with commercial third parties);
    • initiating formal dialogue with commercial publishers and online content providers and learning from their business strategies and experience.

2.3. Responding effectively to rapid technical change

The challenge: Libraries must continually identify and experiment with new tools that promise to extend, enhance, or improve the cost effectiveness, functionality, and educational and cultural value of their services and collections; assess the costs and benefits associated with deploying those tools; and deploy selected tools in their digital library service environments. The effort relies on scarce research and development capacity that needs to be applied across an ever-broadening range of new technologies. It also needs to be applied with greater speed and efficiency. Given the rate and pace of technical change, the time lapse between identification of a potentially valuable new technology and its deployment in a digital library service environment is constantly diminishing and arguably already at only three years. It finally needs to be applied with some urgency. The risks and costs associated with any decision to deploy a new tool with existing collections or services remain stable or are increasing. Accordingly, libraries are investing in more tools, more often, and with less information than at any time in the past.

The strategy: Establish mechanisms that enable members more efficiently, effectively, and meaningfully to share their scarce research and development capacity, and thus to assemble a research and development effort more appropriate to the demands of rapidly changing technical regimes. The following tasks may be associated with this effort:

  1. Creating forums through which research and development staff located at member institutions can effectively inform one another about potentially valuable new tools as they become aware of them and share results of any local experimentation with and assessment of those tools.

  3. Fostering shared experimentation and assessment where appropriate.

  5. Developing guidelines or decision trees that can be used locally to inform evaluation of the different implementation paths that may be deemed appropriate for deploying any new tools in a digital library service environment. Clearly, any work conducted by the DLF within this program area needs to cross fertilize with that conducted elsewhere to achieve greater consistency, predictability, and interoperability within digital library service environments (see Section 2.1. above).

  7. Agreeing on and putting in place open-reference procedures that enable local staff to share applications development work where appropriate

2.4. Penetrating and mobilizing user communities

The challenge: In a digital library, how information is made, assembled into collections, and presented online affects whether, to what extent, and how it can be used. A truism perhaps but one that marks an incremental step for the library into an arena traditionally occupied by the publisher. The statement is not intended to re-open a tired and unhelpful debate about whether the future holds a place for either the library or the publisher. Rather, it demonstrates how our understanding of a digital object's life cycle implies a need to engage differently or, perhaps, just more deeply with our users in order to:
  • enhance our understanding of users' needs, aspirations and behaviors as an essential design component of any online collections and services we may develop; and

  • develop user support services that are appropriate to our evolving digital library service environments and the new and expanded communities that exploit them.

There are at least three additional reasons to place user relations on a revitalized - perhaps slightly different - footing. Some user communities that surround the library are producers of digital content, including research data, dissertations, e-prints, and computer-assisted teaching materials. That content has enormous educational and cultural value, but only if it is assembled into professionally managed collections, maintained over time, and made meaningfully accessible to other end users through online portal and other services. User communities may also possess the expertise or knowledge that, when applied effectively to existing collections can substantially enrich and enhance those collections, lending new meaning to them, even making them accessible or comprehensible to communities for which they were not initially intended. In this regard, engaging with user communities is a vital component of any effective collection development policy.

Second, some user communities are aware of the tools they require to manipulate information and are mobilizing, sometimes on a large scale, to supply those tools - especially where they are unavailable from the commercial sector. Ask a professional archaeologist or a geographer about GIS; a musician or engineer about the manipulation of sound data; an economist, social scientist or astronomer about the management and analysis of large-scale statistical data; a medic or a film producer about the management and analysis of still or moving images. Just as some user communities are poised as net suppliers of digital content, others may be in a position to supply tools to a digital library service environment that can enhance that environment's functionality.

Third, digital library collections and services represent a substantial and growing investment by libraries, educational institutions, and other cultural organizations. Even where there is no commercial imperative to mount such collections and services on a cost recovery or revenue-generating basis, there will be significant pressure to measure performance and value of investment in terms of use. By engaging with user communities more effectively, libraries can inform investment decisions by anticipating their potential benefits (and beneficiaries, where some financial return on investment is sought).

The strategy: Engage more effectively with user communities to build better, more useful, and more useable collections and services. Provide essential data about user markets that may inform decisions about investment in digital library collections or services irrespective of whether any financial return on investment is sought from them. Build sustainable user support services that encourage the use of and are appropriate to our evolving digital library service environments. The following tasks may be associated with this program area:

  1. Developing, documenting, and disseminating quantitative and qualitative methods appropriate to assessing the online information requirements, behaviors and preferences of well defined user communities, and applying these methods in systematically conducted, scientifically controlled, and essentially comparative investigations.

  3. Creating forums through which staff located at member institutions can inform one another and learn from their collective experience in penetrating and mobilizing user communities not only as consumers of digital library services but also as suppliers of content, contextual knowledge, and tools.

  5. Exploring and, where appropriate, developing services, tools, organizational and business mechanisms, and information and training materials that promise cost effectively to:
    • encourage and enrich widespread and appropriate use of digital library service environments;
    • encourage users to supply content, contextual and interpretative knowledge, and tools to digital library service environments; and
    • support the work of reference librarians who mediate between a growing community of users on the one hand and broadening digital library service environments on the other.

2.5. Long-term access to digital information objects

The challenge: After some years of considering digital preservation challenges, strategies and methods, a select few libraries and library organizations appear poised to establish digital archival repositories. The emergence of such repositories suggest that digital libraries are likely to take up to three new roles in some combination: as digital archival repositories; as consumers of digital materials that are supplied by third parties and preserved under license by third party repositories; as creators of digital materials deposited in digital archival repositories for the purposes of their long-term preservation. As they explore the organizational, economic, and legal implications of these new roles, digital libraries will also need to continue research, development, and information sharing in related areas.

The strategy: Support and inform the development of digital archival repositories serving the library community by helping to identify appropriate functional, organizational and economic models for those repositories and the various roles that libraries may play in their development and use. Continue research, development, and information sharing in less understood areas that bear on digital preservation. Tasks that may be conducted in this area are identified below.

  1. With regard to the establishment of digital archival repositories, the DLF membership may wish to help:
    • identify and validate the minimum functional requirements that digital archival repositories must meet if they are to suit the needs and purposes of digital libraries;
    • identify licensing models that appropriately govern the three-way relationships between digital content suppliers, libraries as consumers of digital content, and any digital archival repositories that may emerge to ensure long-term access to such content; and
    • identify accreditation, organizational, and governing models that help engender trust in digital archival repositories, ensure appropriate levels of redundancy in archival effort, and ensure some level of co-ordination across distributed archival repositories.
  2. Where general research, development, and information sharing efforts are concerned, the following emerge from the review as pressing issues.
    • Special collections and archives that continue to accession and hold twentieth-century materials are beginning to explore methods appropriate to the conservation of analog film, sound, and video recordings, and the management of materials accessioned in native digital formats. Their information needs and interests are not yet fully met by digital preservation research and development efforts.
    • Research into the digital preservation strategies and practices that may be appropriate to digital libraries is not yet fully informed by any assessment of the value that digital information holds for those libraries or for the institutions in which they are embedded. Where university libraries are concerned that assessment will arguably require some fuller understanding of the university's disposition towards its information assets as broadly construed and the library's role in helping to manage those assets over the longer term.

2.6. Understanding and influencing new policy and civic contexts

The challenge: By developing digital library service environments the library incrementally but no less fundamentally changes its institutional roles and responsibilities. Delivering digital library services it:

  • has a greater and more direct influence over the nature, character, and possibilities surrounding learning, teaching, and cultural engagement; and
  • challenges traditional relationships that apply within the institution in which it is established and externally with museums, archives and proximate cultural organizations.

Possible developmental trajectories and their organizational, business, and legal implications must to be identified and articulated; they establish a context and a vital orientation for the work required, for example, on digital library architectures, collections, user support and user services, and on digital preservation. Yet this essential framing activity is one that cannot be undertaken by library professionals acting in isolation. Rather, it needs to be conducted through focused cross-party dialogue with others who have an important stake in the emergence of networked information and digital library service environments.

The strategy: Explore and evaluate possible developmental trajectories, roles and responsibilities for the library, and their associated organizational, business, and legal implications. Document and share local experiences and practices that may signpost potentially fruitful and strategic trajectories. Conduct explorations and activities in an essentially cross-party environment. Self-consciously use the results of these explorations to shape the digital library research and development agenda. Possible tasks that may included in this program area include:

  1. Establish cross-party working groups or task forces (at a minimum comprising senior educators and educational administrators) to explore potential roles for the library in:
    • developing, managing, and disseminating educational assets; and
    • supporting computer-based and distance learning and teaching.

  2. Establish cross-sectoral working groups (at a minimum comprising representatives of museums, archives, and library communities) to re-evaluate new roles and responsibilities in an emerging networked civic space.

2.7. Cross-program areas

The challenge: By identifying program areas that reflect communities of interest across the DLF membership, and by providing organizational mechanisms to encourage research, development, and information sharing within those areas the DLF runs a number of related risks. It may:
  • put unnecessary obstacles in the path of important research, development, and other activities that arise outside of or cross program areas;
  • inhibit the emergence of new program areas in response to members' changing needs and interests; and
  • prolong activity in existing program areas beyond its perceived usefulness and value to the membership.
The strategy: Encourage initiatives that emerge from outside of or deal with concerns that cross existing program areas. Ensure periodic review of work undertaken within program areas. Support dialogue and information sharing across program areas. The strategy's success relies heavily on organizational and communications mechanisms that are dealt with in Sections 3.1. and 3.2. respectively. Tasks indicated below are drawn from existing DLF activity whether under way or in preparation. They are indicative of important work that would not be conducted if the DLF adhered to strictly to any programmatic orientation.
  1. Work amongst member text centers has developed agreements pertaining to the use of the Text Encoding Initiative's header that represent a significant step toward greater consistency and interoperability across their collections. They have also prepared implementation guidelines for the TEI's use in different kinds of digitization projects. The text centers are at present developing an agenda that may inform their further collaborative efforts.

  2. Social science data archivists and government data librarians are interested in collaborating to encourage greater interoperability across their distributed collections and a more co-ordinated approach to the development and preservation of collections comprising social science and government data.

  3. The Academic Image Co-operative presents a compelling and potentially very valuable model for developing digital collections that serve specific educational uses in art history. That model needs to be reviewed by a broader group of art historians and visual resource specialists so that it may be elaborated in practice.

3. Organizational and business issues

The challenge: The DLF is a loose confederation of independent libraries co-operating on a broad range of research, development, and other activities to advance their own aims and objectives in establishing digital library service environments. The DLF's program derives its significance, relevance, and potential from one source - the intellectual ownership and direction supplied by its members. The program relies for its fulfillment primarily upon members' professional staff who combine the necessary expertise and experience with a compelling need to share the costs of their digital library research, development and other efforts. It relies for its fulfillment but also for its impact on an effective communications strategy. That strategy must be capable of facilitating the free flow of information upon which any collaborative endeavor will depend and on the summary, synthesis, and dissemination of its results. Given the rate and pace of technical change, and an immature but rapidly evolving understanding of the library's roles and responsibilities within a networked space, the DLF must also remain capable of identifying and responding to new challenges and opportunities as they arise. Finally, the DLF must organize itself and its initiatives with the recognition that it exists outside a hierarchical structure capable of driving and motivating participation in its program and without a budget large enough to drive and motivate participation with the promise of remunerative reward.

The strategy: Create, deploy, and support organizational mechanisms that ensure that the DLF's program is derived from, responsive to, owned by, and realized through its members. Develop and implement a communications strategy to support and encourage the appropriate, wide ranging and free flow of information upon which the DLF's program depends for its development and its impact. Facilitate and motivate shared participation in any applied and practical work associated with the program's development, and reduce the entry and overhead costs associated with such participation.

Tasks associated with this strategy are presented as recommendations to the membership under three heads: organizations, communications, and resources.

3.1. Organizations

The DLF should comprise:
  • a streamlined Executive Committee responsible for policy, programs, and evaluation;

  • Program Advisory Groups which guide and integrate efforts in each of the DLF's core program areas;

  • Task Forces responsible for the focused research, development, and other practical and applied work conducted within program areas;

  • Forums that serve as meeting places and market places for the Executive Committee, the Program Advisory Groups, the Task Forces, and the broader professional community represented in the membership;

  • robust, practical, and mutually advantageous strategic relations with proximate library and other organizations sharing common purposes; and

  • a Secretariat, comprising a few program officers, that is responsible to the membership and will serve a range of inter-related strategic, catalytic, facilitating, supporting, informational, promotional, and business development functions.

These bodies should work to agreed guidelines outlining their respective roles and responsibilities and ensuring that their work promotes the aims of the DLF and its members, and is communicated effectively to the members and the broader community.

The composition, function, and inter-relationships of each of these bodies are outlined in greater detail below.

  1. The Executive Committee The Executive Committee should be responsible to the membership for the Federation's policies, programs, and evaluation, and for the effective delivery of the Secretariat's services. The Executive Committee may:

    • advise on the DLF's policies and program priorities and the allocation of its resources and efforts across its program areas;

    • ensure appropriate levels of cross-fertilization between and among the various program areas;

    • ensure that the work of the DLF is implemented cost effectively and that it provides value for money to the membership;

    • assist the DLF Secretariat, its Program Advisory Groups, and its Task Forces in cultivating advantageous strategic partnerships in any other relevant business development and fund-raising activities;

    • advise the Secretariat and ensure appropriate allocation of its resources;

    • monitor the provision of any services for which the Secretariat is or becomes responsible, using agreed service levels and milestones where appropriate; and

    • assist in the formal evaluation of the DLF and provide advice to the membership and funding partners about continuing the DLF.

    The Committee's size should reflect the need for decisive executive action. Members should include representatives from at least three important constituencies to ensure proper levels of exchange among them:

    • directors of the member institutions (e.g., librarians or their equivalents);

    • chairs of Program Advisory Groups; and

    • a lay constituency drawing participation and perspective from outside the membership or the library sector.

    Mechanisms for electing Executive Committee members and recommendations pertaining to their tenure are left for discussion, as are mechanisms that may ensure the Committee's accountability to the membership.

  3. Program Advisory Groups Program Advisory Groups should be established to guide and integrate the DLF's work in each of its program areas. In this respect, they may:

    • be responsible to the Executive Committee for the aims, objectives, priorities and funds allocated for work within a program area;

    • recommend the establishment of, help prepare the charges for, and allocate funding to any Task Force as may be required to achieve aims and objectives that have been assigned priority;

    • establish and help to implement criteria against which Task Force outputs will be evaluated in the context of the DLF's program objectives and their potential influence on local digital library practices;

    • advise the Secretariat in facilitating, supporting, documenting, and disseminating the learning and other outputs that result from the work of the Task Forces; and

    • advise the Secretariat and the Executive Committee on strategies for promoting, encouraging the adoption of, or otherwise endorsing Task Force outputs, extending their influence, and enhancing their impact.

    Program Advisory Groups should comprise senior professionals (AUL or equivalent and above) from within the DLF who have relevant and demonstrable expertise, creativity, and vision. They should include a chairperson responsible for the Group's strategic development and direction and an individual working full- or part-time on behalf of the DLF Secretariat by supporting the chair, the Advisory Group, and any of its Task Forces in a manner identified in Section 3.1.6., below.

    For the sake of transparency, advisory group members should be selected by the Executive Committee on the basis of expressions of interest submitted to it upon invitation from the Secretariat. Invitations for expressions of interest will at a minimum outline the nature and level of expertise and skills that are sought from advisory group members, the minimum level commitment that is likely to be required of them, and any resource implications that advisory group membership is likely to have for participants' host institutions.

    Given the DLF's strategic reliance upon effective Program Advisory Groups, it may be appropriate to remunerate the chair for his or her service. The matter is left for discussion, as is the size of any Program Advisory Group. In conformance with their recommended role, however, Advisory Groups should seek to involve senior professionals from institutions with a spread of experience and opinion as may be brought to bear fruitfully on the development of any program area.

  5. DLF Task Forces Task Forces should be established to conduct focused research, development, and other practical and applied work as deemed desirable and necessary by the Program Advisory Groups in consultation with the Executive Committee. Task Forces will be responsible to their Program Advisory Group and work to charges developed by the Secretariat under the guidance of that Group. Charges will at a minimum outline the aims, objectives, milestones, and deliverables that are sought from any Task Force and do so with explicit reference to the goals of the broader program area in which it is conceived. They will indicate how work of the Task Force will be assessed, documented, disseminated, and promoted. In this regard, they will clearly indicate milestones in a Task Force's development and the criteria by which it may be deemed to have completed its work. Assessment is intended solely as a means of ensuring formative learning and not as a potentially punitive device.

    For the sake of transparency, Task Force members should be selected by the relevant Program Advisory Group on the basis of expressions of interest submitted to it upon invitation from the Secretariat. Invitations for expressions of interest will at a minimum include the Task Force's charge, outline the nature and level of expertise and skills that are sought from Task Force members, indicate the minimum level commitment likely to be required of Task Force members, and indicate any resource implications that task force membership is likely to have for participants' host institutions.

    Task Forces should consist of small groups of professional staff who have relevant and demonstrable experience and are, within their own institutions, giving priority attention to work in the area being addressed by the Task Force. Task Forces should comprise a chair who is responsible for the work's strategic direction and development, and the individual who is acting on behalf of the DLF Secretariat by supporting work in the relevant program area.

    Given the DLlF's strategic reliance upon effective and productive Task Forces, it may be appropriate to remunerate their chairs. The matter is left for discussion.

  6. DLF Forums Forums will serve as meeting places, as market places, and as congresses. As meeting places they will provide an opportunity for the Steering Committee, Program Advisory Groups, and Task Forces to conduct their business and exchange ideas. As market places, Forums will provide an opportunity for Task Forces and Advisory Groups to present to a larger audience their work, its progress and its likely impacts, and to benefit from the views, perceptions and concerns that may be expressed by that audience. Forums will also provide opportunities for a broader level of information sharing between professional staff and network development among them through thematically organized public presentations and panel discussions, and through informal interchange. As congresses, forums will provide an opportunity for the DLF to continually review and assess it programs and progress with input from the broader professional community.

    Forums should be convened on a regular basis, scheduled and announced substantially in advance, and at a minimum, open to professional staff at DLF member institutions. Accommodation will be made available at Task Forces for face-to-face meetings of Task Forces, Program Advisory Groups and the Executive Committee as required. More public presentations, panel discussions, and opportunities for review of the DLF program may be scheduled by the Secretariat in consultation with Program Advisory Groups and the Steering Committee.

  7. Strategic partnerships Section forthcoming.
  8. The DLF Secretariat The DLF secretariat should be a small, perhaps geographically distributed, body responsible to the DLF membership for the following.

    • Translating members' digital library aims into a shared vision and a program capable of realizing it, and maintaining both in light of the members' changing needs and perceptions of opportunities.

    • Within the context of this program, facilitating meaningful collaboration among the DLF members and between DLF members and other organizations.

    • Supporting and facilitating the work of the DLF's Executive Committee and Program Advisory Groups, ensuring proper levels of communication between and among them.

    • Maximizing the impacts of collaboration initiatives undertaken by Task Forces while lowering their entry and overhead costs by working with Program Advisory Groups and Task Forces to ensure that the Task Forces are:
      • conceived of and directed so as to contribute to the DLF's program and thus its members' institutional aims and objectives;
      • working if not in consultation or liaison with, at least in the full knowledge of other common purpose organizations, initiatives, etc. that are working in complementary areas;
      • effectively and efficiently administered, managed, and properly supported;
      • properly evaluated so as to identify and clearly articulate any contributions they make to the DLF's program and, crucially, to members' own local practices;
      • effectively documented and promoted so as to maximize their impacts and, where appropriate, formally endorsed by the membership so as to maximize their potential influence.

    • Establishing and maintaining strategic alliances with common purpose library and other organizations to achieve economies of scale and to maximize the impact of complementary efforts.

    • Promoting the work of the DLF and its members to the wider community thereby raising the organization's profile and extending the influence of the work conducted by its members.

    • Establishing liaison with strategic and funding bodies and to seek support from them for the DLF's program and its associated dissemination activities.

    The Secretariat should include:

    • a Director responsible to the Executive Committee and the DLF's membership for the Federation's strategic, programmatic, and business development and for the operation of its Secretariat;

    • A Communications Officer responsible for developing and maintaining the DLF's communications infrastructure as documented in Section 3.2. below; and

    • Administrative support at a level appropriate to the nature and scale of the Secretariat's efforts.

    Staff located at or employed by the Secretariat will also support the work of the Program Advisory Groups until such time as the Groups are able to make their own provision for such support. In making that provision, Program Advisory Groups may continue to rely on staff located at or employed by the Secretariat. Funding for all such support will be paid from sums allocated to the Program Advisory Groups by the Executive Committee.

    The Secretariat may also include staff that are employed by and devoted to any DLF initiatives that benefit from outside funding.

    Secretariat staff need not share any central location and may preferably be distributed across member sites.

    It may be desirable, even preferable to secure at least some Secretariat staff on time-limited secondment or release from relevant professional posts.

3.2. Communications

Effective communications are vital to the DLF. They alone can ensure that the DLF's program and program priorities respond to the members' needs and interests. They also form the bedrock upon which any effective and meaningful collaborative engagement will be built. Communications are also essential for the DLF as it sets out to inform its members and the broader library community about the strategies, technologies, organizational mechanisms and legal and business issues that have a direct bearing on the development and cost effective maintenance of high-quality digital library services and collections. Communications are essential to the DLF's own business development. They provide a means by which the DLF can promote itself, its members, and its various activities to the broadest possible community. They also provide a means by which the DLF can extend its influence and its impacts, and in so doing, make the information landscape in which its members operate more consistent and predictable than it is at present. Finally, communications are essential to the DLF as it seeks funding for and participation in its work from other organizations that have a stake in or exercise significant influence over that landscape.

An effective communications infrastructure will serve each of these inter-related needs with minimum tolerable redundancy. It is likely to include the following components.

  1. A Corporate Identity Levering the reputation and importance of its members, the timeliness and significance of its program, and the ability to deliver results in the guise of strategies, practices, and approaches that are not only relevant but influential beyond the narrow community represented by the membership, a corporate identity will help the DLF to:
    • raise awareness about the organization, its members, and its activities;
    • amplify the impact and influence of its activities and outputs; and
    • secure funding and other forms of support for the DLF.

    A corporate identity may be created by:

    • adopting and consistently using the DLF logo in all DLF publications, publicity materials, stationary, etc., whether in electronic or paper-based formats;
    • using a DLF-specific Internet domain for the DLF Web site and for e-mail addresses associated with DLF Secretariat staff members irrespective of where they may be located;
    • consistently applying DLF branding across the DLF Web site; and
    • effectively and consistently cross-referencing the DLF Web site and the site(s) maintained by its members.
  2. A network of electronic mailing lists (with accessible archives)
    • An electronic mailing list open to the public and used primarily for one-way communication between the DLF Secretariat and the broader public announcing the availability of new DLF publications, reports, and other publicly accessible outputs.

    • An electronic mailing list open to staff at DLF member institutions and permitting the one-way communication between the DLF Secretariat and the broader membership, for example to:
      • announce new task forces and advisory groups and seek expressions of interests from potential participants therein;
      • announce new publications and other outputs that result from various DLF initiatives;
      • announce upcoming Forums and other events that are open to staff at DLF institutions; and
      • provide an outlet for information that may be of general interest to those involved in the development and maintenance of digital library service environments that is periodically brought to the attention of the DLF Secretariat by staff at member institutions, by DLF Advisory Groups and Task Forces, or by organizations and individuals outside the DLF.

    • Electronic mailing lists serving specific communities within the DLF (typically created on an ad hoc basis and maintained only so long as they remained useful to their members) and permitting:
      • appropriate and necessary exchange between members of the DLF Executive Committee, its Advisory Groups, and its Task Forces; and
      • information sharing among professional staff working in common areas and interested in focused discussion about issues arising from their work within those areas.

  3. A publicly accessible Web site As the principal means of publication the Web site should provide access to:
    • information about the DLF, its aims, objectives, and program;
    • information about its members including any aspects of their digital library that they wish to emphasize through the DLF; and
    • the publications, reports, tools and other outputs that result from DLF initiatives.
  5. A Web site restricted to staff at DLF member institutions As the principal means of communicating between and facilitating information exchange among the DLF's organizational bodies and its members, and of developing an organizational transaction archive the site should supply:
    • regular updates about work underway in the DLF program areas including progress reports, working papers, etc.;
    • the transaction record of the DLF including agenda, supporting papers, and minutes associated with formal meetings of the Executive Committee, Advisory Groups, and Task Forces; and
    • any Digital Library Resources Reference Database that may be constructed (see Section 3.2.6. below).

  7. DLF publications

    a). Framing the Digital Library. A comprehensive "publication series" would serve as the principal means for disseminating the results of work conducted in DLF program areas. The series may include:

    • Minimum-level content and systems preferences with implementation guidelines that may inform their local application and use (Section 2.1.);
    • Outline collection development strategies including implementation guidelines and decision tools that inform local application and use (Section 2.2.)
    • Recommended practices pertaining to copyright clearance, fair use and other rights and licensing issues as developed conjointly with publishing and academic professional organizations (Section 2.2.);
    • Cost-benefit analyses of alternative business models that may be adopted to sustain specific digital collections including any associated market research data and their analysis (Section 2.2.);
    • Evaluation of the potential and costs involved with new and promising digital library technologies including guidelines and decision trees that may inform their local assessment (Section 2.3.);
    • Brief synopses of the potential strengths, weaknesses, and potential costs Of implementing new technologies in local digital library service environments (Section 2.3.);
    • Open reference source code developed for digital library applications (Section 2.3.);
    • Information about proven quantitative and qualitative methods for assessing the online information requirements, behaviors, and preferences of targeted user communities including application guidelines that may inform local use (Section 2.4.);
    • Substantive publications resulting from user needs' studies (Section 2.4.);
    • Information about services, tools, organizational and business mechanisms, and training and other related materials that promise to enhance and extend user support services offered by the digital library (Section 2.6.); and
    • Policy and position papers enhancing our understanding of the library's roles and responsibilities in a network space, and providing a touch-stone for technical, strategic, and other digital library development work (Section 2.4.).

    2. A DLF Newsletter. A regular Web-based publication targeting a general audience but focusing on professional staff within member institutions, and providing a brief synopsis of recent DLF activities, publications, etc. with pointers to more detailed information where it exists (e.g., on the public or restricted Web sites). A newsletter would benefit enormously from contributions by staff at DLF member institutions, either to promote new digital library services and projects, or to address technical, organizational, business, and other challenges. A newsletter might also provide a vehicle through which the membership could advertise and promote any forthcoming public events that may prove of interest to those involved with digital libraries

    c). DLF Annual Reports. Important for the purposes of accountability, annual reports can also serve useful promotional functions

    d). Printed publicity materials

    • General publicity literature. Targeting general audiences, and disseminated at digital library events hosted by the DLF or its members, and via other means, this literature would highlight the aims and objectives of the DLF as a whole. It would also promote the efforts at each of its member sites. A publicity pack might, for example, enclose single-page leaflets prepared according to a common template but promoting members' distinctive digital library aims, objectives, and achievements).

    • Targeted publicity literature. Targeting specific audiences or focusing on particular DLF projects or program areas, this literature might promote and advance specific causes as may be appropriate to the aims of the DLF.

  8. Digital library resources reference database DLF members want access to digital library expertise and experience. Although there is a marked preference for information that is synthesized and critically evaluated, and thus for the kinds of outputs intended for the Framing the Digital Library publications series, there may be a residual role for some broader resources reference database. Such a database may also evolve as a powerful promotional tool with which members can target highly selected communities to announce, advertise, or seek involvement in their development of new publications, services, or products.

    The costs involved in developing such a database should not be under-estimated, particularly as they will drain effort away from the production of the more desirable publications series referenced above. Accordingly, an incremental approach is advised, one that concentrates initially on sources within DLF member institutions. Categories of information that may be included alongside an indication of how such information may be developed and maintained are given below.

    Information that may be developed and maintained by the DLF Secretariat (in some cases with input from the membership) include the following:

    • membership lists of DLF committees, advisory groups, and task forces
    • a DLF mailing list, including contact details for individuals who attend digital library events sponsored by the DLF or its member organizations or who regularly receive electronic or printed publications that are produced by the DLF or its member institutions
    • a directory of key stakeholders, listing strategically located organizations (and individual contacts within) that are not DLF members, such as funding agencies, government departments, library organizations, and professional societies; and
    • a directory of electronic mailing lists deemed effective in reaching communities with a specific interest in digital library developments

    Information that may be developed and maintained by member institutions (in many cases, information could be entered or mounted by the Secretariat but the member institutions would be responsible for supplying and amending such information:

    • a DLF directory - names, job titles, contact details. Professional staff at DLF member institutions who are willing to supply their names for such a directory could also provide brief descriptions of their digital library expertise and interest;
    • a directory of DLF digital library projects - giving brief details about digital library projects (including contact details) at DLF member institutions; and
    • digital library gray literature (suitably revised for more public presentation), including digital library development and other strategy documents prepared to guide local digital library developments; applications for external funding as sought to support digital library research, development etc.; periodic and summative reports produced by digital library research, development, and other projects and initiatives whether submitted to external funding agencies, local policy or legislative bodies, etc.; working papers, reports, etc. produced and disseminated locally to progress various aspects of members' digital library programs.
  9. Information exchange among the membership Members consistently express a Desire to know more about relevant developments across the DLF and a willingness to supply information about local developments as part of some exchange. Recommendations pertaining to the Digital Library Resources Reference Database arise out of these related interests. Such an exchange would also substantially enhance the work of the Executive Committee, the Program Advisory Groups, the Task Forces, and the Secretariat, ensuring it was well informed by and appropriately responsive to members' needs, interests, and capacities. Facilitating that exchange and, indeed, populating the Reference Database are the key challenges. It is accordingly recommended that DLF members prepare and submit a regular brief report on local activities following a template that is developed to minimize effort in compiling that report while maximizing its usefulness and impact.

In developing the model, the following principals have been applied:

  • The funding model must finally permit a degree of flexibility and in this respect enable the DLF to:
    • alter the pattern and distribution of funding across its program areas; and

The principal costs are those associated with personnel, notably a Director, a Communications Officer, and an Administrative Officer. Additional staff support will be available and organizationally and functionally responsible to the Secretariat but paid for out of budgets allocated by the Executive Committee to the Program Advisory Groups.[4]
  2. Funds allocated to Program Advisory Groups The Executive Committee should allocate funds for the Program Advisory Groups on the basis of bids submitted annually to the Committee. The bids should at a minimum provide for the following:

    • the salary, travel, equipment, and office costs associated with that person or part of a person who is organizationally and functionally responsible to the DLF Secretariat as outlined above. Until June 2001 at least, these functions will be supplied by an existing member of Secretariat staff and paid for with contributions from the Program Advisory Group's budget allocation.

    • any remuneration and travel or other support that the Group may wish to allocate to its chair (within general broad guidelines established by the Executive Committee); and

    • any remuneration and travel or other support that the Group may wish to allocate to Task Force chairs (within general broad guidelines established by the Executive Committee).

    The Executive Committee should only in exceptional circumstances approve funds requested by Program Advisory Groups for:

    • travel costs and associated expenses incurred by staff of DLF member institutions who serve on the Advisory Group or its Task Forces;

    • funding for capital, equipment, and other project costs associated with specific initiatives undertaken by the Task Forces; and

    • funding for activities that would normally be associated with the work of the DLF Secretariat or the member of the Program Advisory Group undertaking functions on behalf of the Secretariat (e.g. administrative support, evaluation, assessment, documentation, dissemination, business development, and other fund-raising activities)

    The above constraints are recommended owing to:

    • The limited size of the DLF's operating budget. A total of only about $550,000 per annum is available for distribution by the Program Advisory Groups and it is assumed, moreover, that the membership will wish to proceed in 3 or 4 program areas.

    • The DLF reliance upon the efforts of small, highly motivated groups of equally expert individuals for whom well facilitated collaboration offers a cost-effective and productive approach to the challenges they are confronting directly and probably in isolation at their own institutions. Funded participation in collaborative initiatives is likely to be differently motivated than is the participation of equally co-investing partners.

    • The DLF's reliance upon its members' willingness to support its initiatives as a crucial measure of the perceived importance of those initiatives.

    • The pressing need for the DLF to invest more than it has in programmatically integrating its various initiatives and in ensuring that those initiatives are properly evaluated, assessed and documented so as to enhance their impact and influence.

    Within and in light of these constraints, Program Advisory Groups should be encouraged to think creatively about developing and providing the necessary resources to their programs. They should be encouraged to take advantage of local opportunities to raise funds and to economize wherever possible (e.g., by combining in the same person the roles and responsibilities associated with the chair and the individual responsible to the DLF Secretariat; by seeking input from Advisory Group and Task Force chairs from appropriately qualified professionals acting on sabbaticals, engaged in fellowship programs, or acting on the bases of releases supported by their host institutions).

  3. External funding for specific DLF initiatives Program Advisory Groups and Task Forces should be encouraged to enlist the DLF's name and the associated reputation of its members to seek external funding for their efforts. To ensure some consistency of approach across program areas and some transparency in any decisions taken to seek external funding, the following guidelines are proposed:

    • The DLF may endorse or play a part in applications for external funding that:
      • promise demonstrably to advance the aims and objectives that are established and agreed to in any of its program areas; and
      • are fundamentally and meaningfully collaborative and include at least two DLF member institutions.
    • In contributing to any funding proposals, the DLF may draw on its developing infrastructure and expertise in any combination of the following ways and help proposed initiatives to:
      • meaningfully, and successfuly collaborate;
      • work within and contribute to the development of a clearly articulated program (the DLF program) designed to enable educational and cultural organizations to take fuller and cost effective advantage of digital resources and information technologies;
      • evaluate and assess themselves and be evaluated and assessed in the context of a larger group (the DLF) and in this respect to achieve a measure of community validation;
      • document any learning and other outcomes, whether derived from the initiative or from its evaluation or assessment, and circulate this document to a broader community; and
      • promote outcomes that can be generalized to a broader community not by their owners or originators, but by an objective third party speaking on behalf of a range of similarly poised and invested institutions.

    • Any contributions supplied by the DLF through its Secretariat or through those taking up the role of the Secretariat within an Advisory Group (whether in project co-ordination, formal evaluation and assessment, documentation, or dissemination) should be supplied on a cost-recovery basis.

    • Funds resulting from successful grant applications should reside with an institution that is responsible for the grant application and not with CLIR (as host for the DLF).
  4. External and additional funding for the DLF program The DLF's program objectives are not uniquely of interest to its members. Rather they promise to supply tools and strategies that will have direct benefit to others who seek educational, cultural, or commercial advantage in digital information and network technologies. There may be particular interest among:

    • Professional curatorial organizations and associations including regional and other consortia of universities, libraries, museums, and archives who share or are beginning to take a strategic interest in research and development agenda which complement that established for the DLF, and may seek to lever complementary investments through some contribution to the DLF;

    • philanthropic, government, and other funding agencies that invest in the creation of digital resources and may have a vested interest in the standards, strategies and tools recommended by the DLF that promise to enhance return on that investment by helping to ensure the usefulness and longevity of those resources; and

    • commercial content suppliers which may have an interest in similar standards, strategies, and tools, but also in any research conducted by the DLF into users' information requirements and online behaviors, into business models appropriate to sustaining digital collections, and into rights, licensing and other issues that may act as impediments to extending their market share

    The DLF Secretariat, supported by the Executive Committee and the Directors of member institutions, should accordingly be encouraged to identify and develop opportunities to enhance core programmatic funding, that is, funding that supports or enhances activities across a range of existing program areas, launches work in new program areas for which there is no available budget allocation, or enhances the contributions that can be made by the Secretariat to the membership as a whole (e.g. any additional or external funding that may be acquired to mount a printed publications program). Any funds resulting from successful grant applications made under this head should be managed by CLIR as host for the DLF.

  5. The DLF membership Core programmatic funding available to the DLF is also available through members' subscriptions and capital contributions. Given the ambitious aims of the DLF's program and the scarcity of funds available to achieve them, the DLF, through its Executive Committee should establish, agree to, and announce a membership policy, including:

    • rates at which subscription fees and capital contributions may be charged;

    • criteria against which membership claims may be evaluated and acted upon; and

    • transparent processes through which applications from any new members may be sought, considered, and acted upon.

    In developing such a policy the DLF should consider the following:

    • There are five organizations with a known interest in becoming members of the DLF and together representing a potential annual capital injection worth $120,000;

    • Given the DLF's current fee structure, the number of potential new members in the DLF is likely to be small and to decline over time;

    • Any potential new members should be evaluated exclusively with respect to their ability to benefit from and contribute to the DLF's stated aims, objectives, and its program. In these regards, they should:
      • be committed to the development of a digital library service environment and host appropriately skilled professional staff able to deliver upon that commitment;
      • be positioned to enhance tangibly the efforts, credibility, influence, or impacts associated with the work emanating from the DLF's program; and
      • be able not only to endorse the DLF's program but also to supply a rational for doing so that is based in local institutional aims and objectives.

    • The DLF's agility in confronting the most challenging research, development, and strategic issues is and is likely to remain more a function of its organization and mission than of its size.

    In developing a membership policy, the Executive Committee might also wish to consider that under the recommendations made in this business plan, the only benefits available to the DLF's current members that are unavailable to non-members are:

    • an ability to influence the program's aims, objectives, and practical direction; and

    • access to materials that are available from the DLF's Web site but restricted to members only (e.g. the DLF's transaction record and progress reports and any digital library resources database that may be constructed).

4. Recommended next steps

The next steps recommend a developmental trajectory for the DLF for the period March 2000 - June 2002. Although appropriate to the program outlined in this document, they may also be generalized. As such, their presence here is not intended to pre-empt any discussion of this document or to forestall any effort to seek revision and amendment, however substantive, to it.

A three-phased approach is recommended to the DLF's development to June 2002:

  • a brief and aggressive re-organizational phase during which essential organizational and communications mechanisms and processes are put into place;
  • a longer developmental phase during which the emphasis shifts to a few highly focused and practical research and development initiatives and their assessment, documentation, and dissemination; and
  • an evaluative phase during which the DLF as a whole is independently and formally evaluated to supply the membership with the data and the recommendations it will in any discussion about whether and how to continue the DLF.

4.1. Phase 1 (April-June 2000). Reorganization

Assuming agreement on the broad recommendations that are presented in this plan, about the program areas that should be selected for early progress, and some putative agreement pertaining to the allocation of DLF funding across program areas, the DLF would seek through its Secretariat to:
  1. Prepare draft remits and broad template guidelines to frame the work of the Executive Committee, the Program Advisory Groups, and the Task Forces.

  3. Establish and convene the Executive Committee to:
    • develop and recommend a membership policy and consider any applications received from potential new members;
    • consider draft remits and template guidelines prepared to frame the work of the Executive Committee, the Program Advisory Groups, and the Task Forces; and
    • consider and advise on approaches to philanthropic and other agencies that may take a programmatic funding interest in the DLF;
    • consider and advise on approaches to organizations that may wish to ally themselves strategically with the DLF.

  5. Establish and convene selected Program Advisory Groups to:
    • refine and develop their program aims and objectives;
    • identify and/or move to recruit appropriate personnel;
    • initiate and prepare charges for Task Forces in areas selected according to the priority assigned to them, their potential for delivering early results (good for morale and also essential to the DLF's profile and credibility), and their ability to serve as a testbed in which the DLF can develop and assess the various organizational, communications, and support mechanisms agreed as part its business plan; and
    • confirm final funding requirements for presentation to the Executive Committee.

  7. Recruit Administrative and Communications Officers for the DLF Secretariat.

  9. Develop a skeletal communications infrastructure including:
    • a DLF domain name;
    • a restricted Web site;
    • DLF stationary as appropriate;
    • selected electronic mailing lists;
    • template reporting forms for use by the membership; and
    • a DLF Newsletter.

  11. Schedule and conduct presentations to potential funders and strategic allies

  13. Program and schedule DLF Forums for the period to June 2002

  15. Convene the Executive Committee to:
    • agree on the membership of and funding for Program Advisory Groups; and
    • review progress of Phase 1 and prospects for Phase 2.

4.2. Phase 2 to December 2001. Program development

  1. Design evaluation plan to be used in assessing the program and possible directions of the DLF.

  3. Develop and implement communications strategy including:
    • comprehensive revision of the DLF Web site;
    • development of general printed publicity material as required;
    • outline, development and launch of "Framing the Digital Library" (possibly to incorporate existing DLF publications in some amended form);
    • development of Digital Library Resources Reference Database; and
    • continuation of the DLF newsletter.

  5. Work in the Program Areas
    • continue work in program areas as advised by Program Advisory Boards; and
    • develop and apply mechanisms for assessing work of the Task Forces and Program Boards to ensure that their contributions to the DLF's program and their potential significance for local digital libraries initiatives identified, documented, and disseminated.

  7. DLF Forum. Organize and host Forums as scheduled.

  9. Executive Committee. Establish Executive Committee meetings on a regular basis with standing as well as arising agenda items.

  11. Establish membership reporting on a regular basis.

  13. Formally report on initial visits to funding agencies; continue routine liaison with funding agencies.

4.3. Phase 3 to June 2002. Review and evaluation

  1. Continue work as indicated under phase 2.

  3. Conduct formal evaluation according to evaluation plan and develop budgets for continuation and exit options.

  5. Review evaluation report and continuation and exit options in plenary meeting of DLF Directors.
