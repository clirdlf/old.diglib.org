Prologue
  • This viewpoint is “anti-silo” but it is not “anti-publisher” at all – it assumes libraries and publishers are part of the same process whose end result must be the betterment of the scholarly enterprise
  • This viewpoint recognizes the value of the well-crafted online product – and its insufficiency if this is the only way that material can be used
  • This viewpoint is biased by my background – the large American university library

Data silos: So what’s the problem?
  • Every publisher is an island; they produce silos of data that plays badly with others – little ability to work cross-publisher and cross-aggregator.  They build product that can only appear on their terms, in their interface.  Leads to passive use.


  • Libraries don’t shelve by publisher and users don’t work this way – runs counter to our normal patterns of behavior.


  • Libraries face a chronic inability to repackage content, especially journals, for local use – we are failing in our service mission to our customers


  • Endless different interfaces; users face time-consuming searches in and out of vendors’ web pages to find material
6
The imagined user is …

  • a) Either someone so specialized that they only need access to one or two silos, and they know what they are.


  •  b) Someone with so much time that they can go in and out of innumerable websites and learn multiple interfaces to pull together their research.


  •  c) A librarian (the publisher’s customer) more than the scholar (the library’s customer).


  • d) Passive – one who wants to look at but not to gather, re-shape, analyze, cross-search, annotate, re-package.
7
What are the users telling us?
  • Dimensions and use of the scholarly information environment -- www.diglib.org/pubs/scholinfo/.  Largest and most current view of American higher education users’ habits.
  • 3,234 hour-long phone surveys of faculty members (930), graduate students (1,056), and undergraduate students (1,248) from over 400 institutions liberal arts colleges to large research universities
8
Electronic content is increasingly important, but print predominates
  • RESEARCH : 35% of the faculty members said they rely exclusively or almost exclusively on electronic sources, with the grad students reporting 49% [Law, 65%] Exception: arts and humanities – 25%


  • TEACHING: About 25% of respondents rely all or most of the time on electronic sources for teaching – Exception: business (42%) and law (30%).


  • About half (49.2%) of undergraduates reported that they used electronic materials exclusively or almost exclusively [business students, 62.9%]
9
Unmet needs and challenges: time, integration, and training hinder use
  • Content : About one-quarter of the faculty members (25.6%) and well over one-third (38.3%) of graduate students expressed a need for more online journals.


  • Time: Nearly 40 percent (38.8%) of the total sample of respondents and 60 percent (60.2%) of the faculty reported "having enough time" as their major problem


  • Retrieval: Respondents expressed frustrations with finding information, determining its credibility, and analyzing it


  • Training: 38.4 percent of respondents saw having insufficient training on how to find information as an impediment.
10
So many silos, so little time
  • What too many users do when faced with library lists of “silos we subscribe to”:  they go to Google, for one-stop shopping and to get a result every time; they go to Amazon, often instead of books in print (and even the OPAC); or they go to their “courseware”  homepage (Blackboard, WebCT) .


  • The library is absent from many of the places our users are going, so the licensed material we provide is too often not found.


  • We ignore convenience at our peril.
11
An illustrative (and unsustainable) problem
  • A faculty member who is building a reading list on a class web page often takes the paper copy of a journal – or asks their e-reserves operation to do the same – and scans in the relevant article even if that material is licensed online.  Why?  Because when he or she goes to a site they are so frustrated by the inability to link at a sufficiently granular level that going back to the paper copy is quicker.


  • Poor interface between library content management systems and courseware; little ability to link from course page to the article level in licensed content


  • This is not a technology problem – it is one of habit, branding, ownership, perceived value, misguided competition, unarticulated needs.
12

Shrinking budgets and rising prices mean that the content needs richer “data behaviors” – it needs to perform in ways closer to the users’ evolving needs.

What do we need to move forward?
  • Malleability: We need the data that resides on publishers’ sites to be much easier for us to re-shape for local customized delivery and analysis. [XML]
  • Multiplicity: PDA, wireless, ebook, text-to-speech, and print-on-demand are all here or coming, and content that can’t go there will under-achieve – it is not just a web world now.
  • We need customer re-shapeable data  to match the delivery format with the immediate needs and location of our users (even when the publisher finds that format uneconomic). [XSLT]
15
What do we need to move forward?
  • Management: Ability for a library to build local services that allow users to interact richly across vendors --  publishers need to help libraries be data aggregation services for the libraries’ customers [OAI; openURL]


  • Mix: we invite our users to visit sites and watch them (TV) ; they want to sample, re-use and re-package as a personal library, a classroom presentation (the music mix)
16
Closing
  • We need to work with the publishers to move towards the promise of “if we build it they will come”.  What is the “it” that we need collectively to build?  Clearly it is not sufficient to offer the current fragmented set of websites defined by a publisher or aggregator.  This frustrates use and wastes time.
  • We need to evolve away from the current situation to one where the library and the scholar can mix and match across silos, and can create new views ordered by criteria other than publisher/aggregator.
  • We need data that behaves freely within its licensed user community, to encourage innovative use, and to recognize the pressures of time we labor under.
17
Closing
  • Data that play well with others


  • Innovative users need innovative data


  • Time/convenience are major “use forces”


  • We need to mix and match, and reshape for local needs


  • The current model cannot sustain itself – branding and data security need to be separated from a monolithic website
