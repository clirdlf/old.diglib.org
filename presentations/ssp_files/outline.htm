2
Background
  • Preservation and Access – in the world of physical documents:


    • The worst thing for preservation is access
    • Archives are safest when they are never used
    • Benign neglect aids physical preservation
3
Background
  • In the digital world, the reverse is true – access aids archiving.
  • That which will survive will be :
    • content that is easily re-shaped (non-proprietary and standardized format; control vocabularies);
    • content that is seen to be useful and that functions well
4
Archiving & preservation: Who?
  • Traditionally, libraries, museums, and archives


  • Is long-term preservation a new role or publishers?  A welcome change?  Can the costs and benefits be adequately articulated in the publishers’ product planning and pricing structures?


  • Can we conceive of new consortia who will maintain computer files for a larger group of institutions?
5
Archiving & preservation : How long?
  • Educational content typically has an academic life much longer than it has a commercial life
  • University libraries are less ephemeral than publishers over the centuries, and may invest in preservation of content longer than publishers are willing to do so.
  • So – as we look forwards and back, let’s look at what the data needs to be (standards for creation and migration) but also at  how it needs to behave (function) in order to maximize its chance of being worthy of archiving.
6
Looking Back
  • Let’s glimpse back into our dark primordial past as purveyors and consumers of digital content:
  • In the early 1990s, academic libraries persuaded publishers of early digital library content to provide that material on tape for local loading or online, rather than on CD-Rom disks.  Why?
  • Minor reason: solved by online access from a publisher’s site: it was inconvenient to have to go to a physical building to use a digital library item, which was capable of traveling to you across a network.
7
Looking Back
  • Middling reason: not solved by online access from a publisher’s site: a collection of CD-Rom disks was inappropriate to research and teaching needs because each product had an interface that was different in aesthetics, logic, and function to the next. This situation produces an enormous training burden for the library and leads to low use/high user frustration.
  • Major reason: not solved by online access from a publisher’s site: each CD-Rom is isolated from related data not on that CD.  Every publisher is an island, producing data that plays badly with others.
8
 Looking back
  • By moving content online that still has some of the bad habits of the CD-Rom era we have simply made it more convenient to get to the core problems.  Now you can be frustrated from your office and from home.


  • Is there a real danger that today’s web-based content –the non-interoperable data silos run by publishers— will go the way of the CD of a decade ago?
9
What do we need for Utility now and Archiving later?
  • Persistence –content that we rely on needs to be available to us in (say) 30 years – when today’s undergraduates are professors.


  • Do publishers really want this burden?


  • Publishers let things go out of print.  Publishers do not have preservation departments. Can publishers change their behavior and skills set radically, in order to assume the preservation roles of the library?
10
 What do we need?
  • Partnerships -- We need some commonality of descriptive metadata across publishers and a much greater understanding by publishers of the libraries’ challenges – and vice versa.  We need to find forums in which we can work collaboratively on these shared problems.
  • Aggregation -- We need the ability to aggregate content from one publisher and journal with content from other publishers and journals. We need the ability to deal with material at an appropriate level of granularity – for a journal, this is the article at the very least.
11
 What do we need??
  • Malleability -- We need the ability to match the delivery format with the needs and location of the user – these needs are not always served by using a web browser on a desktop machine with a large screen and a T1 connection.


  • PDA, wireless, ebook, and print-on-demand are all coming at us, and content that can’t go there will be as dead to general use as the microfilm collection, or CD-bound content.
12
An Illustrative problem
  • Publishers build websites for journal content.  Libraries license them.  Libraries puts up websites of links to journals to which they subscribe, often arranged alphabetically.
  • The imagined user is:
  • a) Either someone so specialized that they only need access to one or two journals or sites, and they know what they are.
  •  b) Someone with so much time that they can go in and out of innumerable data silos and learn multiple interface.
  •  c) A librarian.
13
An Illustrative problem
  • What too many users do:  They go to Google, for one-stop shopping and to get a result every time; and they go to their class homepage in Blackboard or WebCT.  The library is absent from most of the places, so the licensed material we provide is too often not found.  “If we build it we will come”.
  • A faculty member who is building a reading list on a class web page often takes the paper copy of a journal and scans in the relevant article even if that material is licensed online.  Why?  Because when he or she goes to a site they are so frustrated by the inability to link at a sufficiently granular level that going back to the paper copy is quicker.
14
Solutions
  • The same standards and practices that fix these issues of near-term access also greatly aid archiving, re-use, and survival.
  • Institutional repositories research, and involvement with national preservation initiatives: National Digital Preservation -- $150 million+
  • Xml core documents that can be transformed to meet the users’ changing needs through XSLT (web, ebook, PDA) and FOP.
  • Professional and reliable cataloging – name authority files in particular.
15
Solutions
  • Open Archives Initiatives (OAI) records -- at the article level – arguably the single best thing that publishers could do today to improve the libraries’ ability to re-shape the delivery of the content for local needs.
  • Shibboleth – for much greater authentication flexibility – greatly in our joint interest for appropriate digital rights management – DRM as an enabling force for scholarly enquiry, rather than a punitive one.
16
Conclusions
  • We need the data that resides on a publishers’ site to be much easier for us to re-shape for local delivery. Publishers need to help their customers -- libraries – market the publishers’ data to the faculty and students we serve. When we all have much the same content, better service is what distinguishes a library.
  • The standardized and malleable document – that which survives is likely to be that which is easily migrated, and that which supports a sufficient range of re-use to keep it economically viable.
  • We need to move from data silos defined by publisher to data that can be deeply shared across publishers.
