We’ll start with a brief review of where RLG and the institutions in the Cultural Materials Alliance are today in aggregating their digitized special collections to make them easily accessible for teaching and research. 
The majority of the talk will focus on the next phase of the initiative.  This will include
The model that guided the investigation
the results of RLG's investigation of image stock-houses,
the plans for open web access via internet search engines,
and the results of the deliberations of the Alliance policy advisory group.
There are now 53 institutions in the Alliance. You can see the significant amount of overlap with DLF institutions.  85 collections, another 179 proposed.  207K works, representing well over half a million digital files. We hope that DODL will complement the Cultural Materials Initiative and develop some mutually benefical synergy.
The Alliance formed to:
Leverage expertise among libraries, archives, museums to address common issues collaboratively
Identify and promote best practices
Bring together digital surrogates of research materials
Make them accessible for research and learning. 
The academic service is made available by institutional subscription.
We’ve addressed many challenges:
--In addition to supporting digital representations of images, documents, audio files, video clips, we have implemented a METS viewer to allow for navigation of complex digital objects. --In aggregating the records created by diverse descriptive practices, we’ve combined different formats, different approaches, different vocabularies, different levels of cataloging, and very thick and very thin records.  The greatest amount of effort is spent analyzing each collection, mapping it to our scheme, and doing some targeted normalization. --Meaningful integration across collections required identifying common access points, as well as making record displays and surrogate viewing as consistent as possible. --Building a reliable infrastructure was a real challenge because we were working in a brand new environment, learning new skills as we went:
IBM DB2 and WebSphere,
writing routines (with colorful names like CultureVulture and FotoRooter) to harvest images and create derivatives. establishing a Hierarchical Storage Management system and a new authentication server --In order for the service to be useful in academia, it was important to have consistent terms and conditions.  The Policy Advisory Group led the Alliance in coming up with agreements as to institutional rights and responsibilities --And the biggest ongoing challenge is content development.  As you know, institutions are pulled in many directions.  Even though we make it as easy as possible to contribute collections, it takes an army of nice, but needling, RLG staff and a bunch of responsive and engaged member staff to keep the resource growing.
Contributors can provide data on
•their own server and we’ll get it (via FTP, HTTP, or OAI)
•RLG’s FTP server, or on
•120 GB and 240 GB hard disks [The postal system has surprisingly large bandwidth – though throughput can be very slow.]
Wildly heterogenous descriptive data is analyzed and mapped, converted to XML, and transformed to the Cultural Materials schema.
Images are converted to MrSID and JPEG service copies of various resolutions.  The TransValidator routine ensures that the descriptive data links to the digital surrogates and that the digital surrogates are linked to by descriptive data.  We had a 90 minute staff presentation on all the derivation and validation processes involved.  90 minutes was not enough for just an overview.
Complex objects are converted to METS (we can accept METS, too) to lash them together and allow for meaningful navigation.
And then everything is loaded into a test database for several stages of review.
Meeting all these challenges has resulted in
The provision of access to digital objects documenting civilization – often rare, fragile, or hidden treasures,
Integrated access across diverse descriptive practices,
Access to digital surrogates of materials in all formats,
Cross-collection searching, and
Presenting relationships among digital objects and their context
The resulting aggregation culminates in a manifestation called the Result Overview …
… which, in this result set of works containing the keyword “hunting”, categorizes a result set into views of
-- “what” it is (type of works, collections, subjects, media)
-- “where” a work was created or depicted
-- “who” created the work, is depicted in the work, or contributed the work.
The result overview of a set represents one  way of looking at….
… a diverse array of fascinating content – on almost any topic.
Now we’ll switch from past tense to present and future tense.
The next phase of the Cultural Materials Initiative is to take what we’ve built for the academic community and repurpose it to meet a variety of goals:
--reaching new audiences,
--providing broader awareness of and access to the institutions' special collections,
--and testing the waters of commercial licensing. 
There were several key areas of needed expertise that were outside of our normal practice, so it seemed a good candidate for some outsourcing.
The working name for this service is Trove.  The academic subscription service is hereafter referred to as RCM.
We started with a model for reaching a variety of users with a variety of services. 
The Hub represents a single instantiation of each object to serve all the needs of the services.
Users can find Trove content by visiting:
--Our dedicated site, called here the Trove portal or through
--and others’ sites that feature links to our content.
But the majority will likely come through the search engine of their choice.
They find an item in Yahoo for instance which brings them into the hub.
In the Hub, items are associated with particular services (the spokes to the right)
Choosing a service takes the user off to make a transaction of one kind or another.
I’ll gloss the components of the model starting with the hub.
It’s important to have one place where all the Trove content is accessible in order to include all contributors.  No one visits the hub directly.  Rather they follow a link to a specific item.
The Hub will also be the supply depot for any partners who need to load our data in their own systems.
Optimizing for internet indexing ensures the widest possible audience and precludes the need to develop a separate search engine and interface.
Connecting at the item level to spoke services allows the users (whether consumer or professional) to choose from the services available for that particular item.
Redirecting to the Trove portal is important.  This is the way to search all Trove content and only Trove content.  And visitorship will encourage sponsorship.
This shows a sample Hub item.
The services for a given item are shown.
All items link to the surrogate repository.
Every item offers:
An opportunity to go to the Trove portal
An opportunity to go to the subscription service (for authorized users).
A licensing opportunity
some items will offer:
Additional licensing opportunities based on cherry picking by other stockhouses
print options, and other services
The search engines are, for most users, the way users find Trove content.  They are also the primary means for marketing Trove to the general public.
From some services (e.g., Google images), users may be able to go directly to spokes.  From the rest, they’d be referred to the item in the Hub and from there make a selection.
Utilizing the existing internet search engines has these benefits:
Makes Trove available to users where they usually look
No need to publicize a separate site
No need to establish a separate search engine and interface.
This is a screen grab of a Google Image search result.  A Trove item has been pasted in at the top of the list. This one and two other images offer “more results from x” – the others are from American Memory and The City Museum of NY.  In each case you get your same search limited to that domain name.  One way to offer “more like this”!
Selecting our fake item gives you all the spoke choices, clickable right from within Google Images. Our links within the description (e.g., automobile dealerships) takes you to a further result set within Trove content.
The portal is the place to which RCM contributors can link from their sites to refer their users to their content in the context of other collections.
The portal is the place where users can search all Trove and only Trove content.
The portal is the place where we collect eyeball counts for potential sponsors
The portal is really just a pretty face for the Hub – perhaps a single HTML page (or a few describing the content) The portal merely uses one of the Internet Search Engines with a limit to things in the Hub. When users go to the Trove portal, they can be redirected to the subscription service if they are authorized.
The Trove portal is simple.
A search box
Access to some info for them what wants it
Perhaps a sponsor logo. [unfortunately Levi Straus has not yet generously sponsored the site]
Those running other portals may
come to us or they may
act as a portal without our knowledge
or be the result of a promotional campaign to get more sites referring to ours.
They may
link to the Trove portal
offer filtered searching of an internet search engine,
or make available on their site links to selected Hub items.
Redirection to the subscription service is important, since many users at subscribing institutions have no idea that they have access to RCM (or that it exists!).  They can stumble onto something on the web and be drawn into it.  [Conversely, the subscription service will refer subscription users who want to use the materials in ways not covered by their educational license to the Hub to obtain spoke services.]
Since many users go to the open web as a first resort, Trove content will be visible, but then we’ll lead them into the richer content and improved discovery that their institution makes available to them.
Professional stock houses will want to select images to offer in their services.  Stock houses have established markets and will select images that they believe will appeal to their markets. Some images will be available through more than one stock house.
These selected images will likely be ingested into their systems in accordance with the rights/terms established by the Alliance.  We’ll receive a portion of the licensing fee to share directly with the contributor.
Most stock house clients will find our content through the stock house interface.
Items are available via various e-commerce opportunities.
A request comes via the Hub with an identified item that contains a link to the repository.  This spoke, offers options, completes the financial transaction, and OKs order fulfillment.
This is an example of an e-commerce opportunity.  There may be one or more of these spokes.
These may be popular with users and therefore significant revenue generators.
So that was the vision.  Now the reality (so far)
After months of scouring the landscape, courting possible partners, receiving proposals and presentations, winnowing and focusing… We are now negotiating with stockhouses.  We also investigated a number of potential partners for building the hub site and brokering the many relationships needed to make it prosper.
We’re beginning with a negotiation with a stockhouse that is willing to take on all of our content in a non-exclusive arrangement, so that other stockhouses can represent Trove content, too.
RLG will export works as XML records and a concordance linking them to form the initial hub site.   Stockhouses will receive the data they require from the hub site.  RLG will optimize Hub content for Internet search engines spidering.
We’ve decided to start with a modest version of the hub model to allow us to experiment and prototype while we explore building it out with all the requisite relationships.
From the very beginning, the Policy Advisory Group has made each step possible
They determined terms and conditions of:
alliance participation
subscription use and terms
Criteria for non-member contributions sponsored by an Alliance participant
And are working out the terms and conditions for this next phase
So far, they have
Agreed upon uses for licensed materials
Acceptable fees for such licensing
And a means for institutions to opt in or out at the collection level
And once we’ve finished the stockhouse negotiation, they’ll agree on an approach to revenue sharing for licensed content.
There’s been a lot of ground covered by the Alliance – and progress made, particularly in these areas:
-- ease of harvest
-- aggregation of disparate content
-- a rights management frame work
-- terms and conditions for academic and research use
-- self-sustainability
-- reaching new audiences
-- testing the waters of commercial licensing
We hope that what we’ve learned will inform other efforts to help make special collections more widely accessible.
This is pretty exciting – we’re investigating areas of importance to the contributors (new audiences, broader awareness of their institutions and collections, and new revenue sources) and RLG is exploring new models for making research resources more affordable and accessible, new opportunities for sustainability, and new ways forward.
