CrossRef is a collaborative linking network and the members are primary publishers.  However, libraries and secondary publishers also use the CrossRef system.  We are increasingly collaborating with these organizations.
For those of you that don’t know, I just want to describe briefly what CrossRef does.   CrossRef, as an IDF registration agency, uses the DOI system and its own system, to make linking between full text scholarly journal articles efficient, manageable and reliable.  The links are between journals but also from secondary databases and from library systems and content.
The links out from journals add value to those journals and inbound links drive traffic to publishers’ sites.  But CrossRef is moving beyond journals and our mission is to be the complete citation linking backbone for all scholarly literature in electronic form.
Now, I want to show you CrossRef in action.  I have screenshots of HTML pages that I went to on Friday last week to demonstrate CrossRef in practice.  First of all we start in an article in Science. 
If we go to the references, we can see many CrossRef links – Science sent these references to CrossRef to look up the DOIs.  Let’s take a look at reference 6 and click it.
We arrive at the abstract for the article in Trends in Genetics.
We can go down the the references in this article and see even more CrossRef links.  If we go to reference link 42 we can click that and we go immediately to Wiley Interscience to the abstract for that article.
So the main question is – is CrossRef working?  The answer is a resounding yes.  There are 83 member publishers and 20 libraries and affiliates.  Over 3.5 million articles have been registered from over 5,000 journals.  More than 80% of the publishers are depositing and a growing are linking.
More importantly and increasing number of CrossRef DOIs are in journal article references and users are clicking them.  The DOI Foundation provides statistics on the number of DOI resolutions and there were almost 1 million in July and 8,000,000 in September.  Some of this is increased usage, but a lot of it is also testing – for instance 3,000,000 resolutions of one DOI occurred in one day.  This level of resolutions really put the DOI system through its paces and it performed very well.
CrossRef is on track to registered between 500,000 and 1 million articles per year.
And as Norman talked about, Multiple Resolution is a key development.  CrossRef is looking at many areas where this could add value for journals.  Tomorrow’s IDF session was billed as using the DOI for commercials transactions and said “turn every DOI into a sales opportunity” – from CrossRef’s perspective, this is a little too narrow.  Multiple resolution will make linking more sophisticated and can be used in many different ways – mirrors copies of articles, different versions or rights information about an item.
I will be giving a full demonstration tomorrow but just wanted to quickly show multiple resolution attached to journal articles.
