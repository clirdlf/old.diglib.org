With apologies to Thomas Friedman, the world may be flat, but the complex relationships among material culture images are not. Historically, repositories were  expressed through drawer arrangement

In the beginning of this year, two Harvard libraries (Fine Arts and the Graduate School of Design) opted to undertake an ARTstor hosting project, where records from Harvard’s visual image union catalog (called VIA) would also be made available in ARTstor. This allows faculty and students to take advantage of the presentation capabilities of ARTstor’s offline image viewer and of the convenience of searching Harvard images together with those in other ARTstor collections. However, the difference in record structures between the two environments required significant conversion – work that ARTstor took on -- and will require a special approach to maintenance as updated records are reported over time. Emerson Morgan from ARTstor and I want to share some of our experience taking metadata from a loosely-typed hierarchical record structure and transferring it into a flat record environment.

Even when they are relational behind the scenes, the public view of image catalogs is often self-contained and flat: one record per image.  For users, it can be a very good thing, letting them eyeball a number of images to decide which one they want. When image databases get large, however, there can be dozens, hundreds, or thousands of images of a single artwork or building or site, and retrieval sets can be unwieldy.
