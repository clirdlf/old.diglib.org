In the beginning of this year, two Harvard libraries (Fine Arts and the Graduate School of Design) opted to undertake an ARTstor hosting project, where records from Harvard’s visual image union catalog (called VIA) would also be made available in ARTstor. This allows faculty and students to take advantage of the presentation capabilities of ARTstor’s offline image viewer and of the convenience of searching Harvard images together with those in other ARTstor collections. However, the difference in record structures between the two environments required significant conversion – work that ARTstor took on -- and will require a special approach to maintenance as updated records are reported over time. Emerson Morgan from ARTstor and I want to share some of our experience taking metadata from a loosely-typed hierarchical record structure and transferring it into a flat record environment.
Even when they are relational behind the scenes, the public view of image catalogs is often self-contained and flat: one record per image.  For users, it can be a very good thing, letting them eyeball a number of images to decide which one they want. When image databases get large, however, there can be dozens, hundreds, or thousands of images of a single artwork or building or site, and retrieval sets can be unwieldy. 
The world of metadata standards and formats has been moving so fast, it’s hard to remember what it was like even 8 years ago. The prime rationale for VIA, when it was designed in 1998, was to make it possible to discover together visual resources scattered across collections at Harvard: collections that had no web presence at that time and had no institutional mandate to speak to each other: independently administered libraries, museums, and archives. Given the intramural work needed to get VIA off the ground, and the limited development of standards and systems in the visual resource community at that time, eventual sharing of VIA metadata with the wider community was entirely theoretical. And as sometimes happens, cooperating with ourselves was challenge enough. The point being that we had an immediate problem to solve, with technologies available to us, and the VIA record structure reflects that.
Contributing museum databases often had multiple images for a given work of art. Archival collections were cataloging photographs at the folder level, with only a few data elements at the individual image level, so we knew we needed something more than a flat record. Contributing repositories compromised on a generalized target metadata format that drew on VRA Core Categories 2.0, CDWA, Dublin Core, MARC and other schemes, but that was, ultimately, our own: a single set of repeatable elements that could be applied at any of three levels: Individual Works,
Surrogates, and
Groups of Works.
This structure was flexible enough that museums, archives, and art and architecture study collections could all manipulate their legacy records to fit.
So how is this relevant today? Harvard’s source metadata may be decidedly shabby and old-school at this point, but aspects of the record structure are in some ways forebears of the very newest standards for visual resource metadata: VRA Core Categories 4.0 and the Cataloging Cultural Objects descriptive guidelines. VRA 4.0, which is still not formally released, and CCO, published last summer, both define three record types: Collection, Work, and Image. CCO goes so far as to state “Records for individual works or images can be hierarchically linked as part of the group, collection, or series record.”  Harvard’s groups, works, and surrogates are not so different from the VRA and CCO record type concepts of collection, work, and image.
The key differences are that 1) in VIA records related descriptions are encapsulated in a single record and 2) the relationships among descriptions are loosely rather than strictly typed.
VRA and CCO have developed extensive lists of the kinds of relationships that can exist between collections, works, and images. CCO has gone a step further and categorized the relationship types. We will learn more about how this works once a body of records is created using VRA 4.0 and CCO, and organizations begin exchanging them. In VRA 4, each level of description is an independent record, and these relation elements and types will drive system functionality to associate them for retrieval and display and exchange.

I’ve consciously talked about VIA record structure rather than data model, because on Harvard’s side, this was a very pragmatic way for catalogers to apply an organization to a set of materials. The structure is flexible but consciously limited:
It does not rigorously type relationships among objects or images. For example, images of a whole work and images of details are siblings in our records: they are all just representations of the work. In cases like Fallingwater, librarians chose to create multiple records applying an organization to the materials. It is arbitrarily limited to three tiers (groups of works, individual works, surrogates). Links to digital images can occur at any of the three levels.
It is arbitrarily limited to 300 children per parent.

ARTstor had to merge values from different hierarchical levels into a single level of elements, concatenating values (such as Title) or repeating elements (such as Subject) as necessary.
All the retrieval vocabulary is retained in the ARTstor record, but the associations – which values apply to the group, which to the work, and which to the surrogate— are lost.
