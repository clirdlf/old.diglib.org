VIA Record Permutations
The world of metadata standards and formats has been moving so fast, it’s hard to remember what it was like even 8 years ago. The prime rationale for VIA, when it was designed in 1998, was to make it possible to discover together visual resources scattered across collections at Harvard: collections that had no web presence at that time and had no institutional mandate to speak to each other: independently administered libraries, museums, and archives. Given the intramural work needed to get VIA off the ground, and the limited development of standards and systems in the visual resource community at that time, eventual sharing of VIA metadata with the wider community was entirely theoretical. And as sometimes happens, cooperating with ourselves was challenge enough. The point being that we had an immediate problem to solve, with technologies available to us, and the VIA record structure reflects that.

Contributing museum databases often had multiple images for a given work of art. Archival collections were cataloging photographs at the folder level, with only a few data elements at the individual image level, so we knew we needed something more than a flat record. Contributing repositories compromised on a generalized target metadata format that drew on VRA Core Categories 2.0, CDWA, Dublin Core, MARC and other schemes, but that was, ultimately, our own: a single set of repeatable elements that could be applied at any of three levels: Individual Works,

Surrogates, and

Groups of Works.

This structure was flexible enough that museums, archives, and art and architecture study collections could all manipulate their legacy records to fit.
