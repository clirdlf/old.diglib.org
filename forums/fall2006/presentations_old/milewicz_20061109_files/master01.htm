What is the database?
-CD-ROM database, published in 1999
-numerical humanities database, built through collaboration of historians researching the trans-Atlantic slave trade -Over 27,000 records of separate slave-trade voyages (will increase to 35,000); accounts for app. 2/3-3/4 of voyages that crossed Atl (1500s-1800s) -over 500 variables--both qualitative & quantitative-- used to capture details of the voyages
(mortality of crew & enslaved Africans, ports of embarking & disembarking, etc.) -Constructed between 1993 & 1997, under sponsorship of the WEB Du Bois Institute for African and African American Research,
with funding from the National Endowment for the Humanities, the Mellon Foundation, and the Ford Foundation
to gather data, build database, and publish as CD-ROM
 Queen’s University professor David Eltis co-led the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database project—a collaborative effort among international slave-trade researchers to pool existing datasets (both published and unpublished) and newly discovered data into a single, searchable database.
The resulting Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database represented decades of accumulated scholarly research – data gathered through years of scouring historical government and business records of the slave trade, in archives and cultural institutions in South and North America, Europe, and Africa. These scholars’ collaboration to create the database resulted in a single, searchable view of data on the trans-Atlantic slave trade, which in turn has allowed for more expansive research into other areas of historical research: for instance, into economic relationships between Europe and the Americas (measured through data on ship construction and ownership) and the implications of African diet on mortality (measured through data on slave mortality and port of embarkation).
Limitations:
-costly resource (used mostly by professional researchers who access it through academic institutions)
- static resource -  costly and difficult to update
- no system for accepting and reviewing new data submissions, further complicating the long-term viability of this resource

SUSTAINABLE
Typical understanding: what do we do when the grant money runs out? How do we help this resource run itself?
A more expansive understanding: not just a concern with where money comes from after sponsored project end or how to reduce the expense of running the resource, but also how to ensure the long-term integrity and utility of a resource among its original audience. A concern not just for the money that keeps a resource viable but also a concern for the users who keep a resource viable by contributing content, time, interest -- often as a reflection of the value of that resource to them as a source of information and as a tool.
Some answers here, but not all: we face common struggles as we move forward, from sponsored project to self-sustaining resource. I’ll present how we are addressing these questions, and also note where answers are yet incomplete.
In general, our strategy for addressing these questions is to build this project through close COLLABORATION with the scholars already vested in its maintenance and growth.
That is certainly a concern for this project, and one that collaboration helps to address. In addition to plans to establish an endowment for covering routine maintenance and updating costs, the use of an online data entry form and the creation of an editorial distribution system and of a digital preservation network will reduce the time and effort previously involved in vetting and sharing new data and will allow the work of updating and maintaining the integrity of the database to be shared.

1 - ANSWER: acknowledging distinct roles IS part of maintaining the collaboration. Praising and publicizing others’ unique efforts towards the greater goal contributes to the culture of collaboration, in which individuals perceive that their work is valued and their contribution to the project, product, or process matters. Not acknowledging distinct roles or the collaborative partnerships--essentially taking credit for shared work--does undermine collaboration. In the case of the Voyages project, individuals and institutions contribute differently and to different degrees.
2 - ANSWER: Just as an understanding and acknowledgment of roles and responsibilities contributes to collaboration, a shared understanding of the purpose of the collaboration, along with some rules for interaction, can help partners both contribute to the project and compromise on directions and outcomes. Working day-to-day with scholars has meant more discussion than usual around design questions, with all partners forced to explain & support their opinions on the database development. Aside from helping everyone to understand the full implications of the design choice, this discussion provides an opportunity to re-articulate the purpose of the collaboration. Just as acknowledging roles helps support the collaboration, the shared sense of purpose can help align expectations and move the project forward. But if partners think their own concerns or objectives outweigh those of others, the project will get stuck. The Voyages project benefits from a foundation in collaboration and shared purpose. The original authors of the database, and the other researchers who contributed data, believed in the importance of putting this data in one place more than in their own accolades for contributing research; likewise, they recognized that individual, isolated effort would not gain them the benefits that collaboration would. By pooling their resources, they all created and benefited from a more powerful resource. The Voyages project goals were established by the researchers and align with those of digital librarians, and help to guide the project toward a common purpose.

3 - ANSWER: Money drives much of what we do or don’t do, what we accomplish and what doesn’t get done. The reality of scarce resources (people and time as well as money) drives our search for voluntary effort even as it limits our ability to freely volunteer our own resources for projects we care about. So, there must be other benefits, some other currency that compensates our and others’ investments. Praise and prestige are rewards, of course, and one way of both motivating participation and rewarding contributions. Exchanging critical information and tools is another strategy for building mutually beneficial partnerships. And there are other rewards, depending on the groups. Again, the Voyages project began somewhat advantageously, with its basis in scholarly exchange. Contributing to the development of the original database benefited all partners as well. As this project moves forward, it will continue the practice of acknowledging contributors of new data and scholarship (e.g., through established “Editors” variables). A challenge we face now and in the future is how to continue development of the K-12 curricular materials--lesson plans, teaching guides, and activities, plus additional content that helps students connect with the database.  Part of building future partnerships on limited resources is identifying crucial needs that participation fills.
