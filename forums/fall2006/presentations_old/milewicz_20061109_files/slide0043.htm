Collaborative practices
3.How do we encourage participation from existing and potential partners if there is little monetary incentive to do so?
¡Identify critical needs that participation fills
¡Find commonalities in mission, work
¡Publicly acknowledge contribution
3 - ANSWER: Money drives much of what we do or don’t do, what we accomplish and what doesn’t get done. The reality of scarce resources (people and time as well as money) drives our search for voluntary effort even as it limits our ability to freely volunteer our own resources for projects we care about. So, there must be other benefits, some other currency that compensates our and others’ investments. Praise and prestige are rewards, of course, and one way of both motivating participation and rewarding contributions. Exchanging critical information and tools is another strategy for building mutually beneficial partnerships. And there are other rewards, depending on the groups.
Again, the Voyages project began somewhat advantageously, with its basis in scholarly exchange. Contributing to the development of the original database benefited all partners as well. As this project moves forward, it will continue the practice of acknowledging contributors of new data and scholarship (e.g., through established “Editors” variables). A challenge we face now and in the future is how to continue development of the K-12 curricular materials--lesson plans, teaching guides, and activities, plus additional content that helps students connect with the database.  Part of building future partnerships on limited resources is identifying crucial needs that participation fills.
