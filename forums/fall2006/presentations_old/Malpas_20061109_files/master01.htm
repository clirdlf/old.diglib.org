What do we mean by the “collective collection”?
Over the next few years libraries will devote more attention to system-wide optimization of collections - within consortia, regions or countries. We have been using the phrase 'collective collection' to evoke the more focused attention to coordinated development, management and disclosure of collections this suggests
•The term was first applied (by LD, ca August 2005) to describe the system-wide print collection, typically as it is represented in WorldCat – which is far from comprehensive, but nevertheless represents more than 32M book titles (or 26M works) held in >20K libraries worldwide.
•With the advent of the Google Book Search project, some commentators have envisioned the creation of a universally accessible online library holding, if not all printed works, at least a core set of texts representing the holdings of several major print collections.  The Anatomy of Aggregate Collections suggested that the “wholesale” selection methods of the Google Print project might be more successful in creating a representative core than some imagined, since there is less overlap between research library holdings than is typically thought.  Open access projects like the Open Content Alliance fueled hopes that mass digitization might actually produce an aggregate collection that would permit creative recombination and re-use.   Kevin Kelly, editor of Wired magazine, brought this message to the masses in a widely-read piece published in the New York Times in May 2006. 
•But as Jean-Noel Jeanneney, director of the Bibliotheque nationale de France, reminded us (Jan 2005; English version 2006) what constitutes a “universal library” depends on one’s cultural and geographic context.  2005 was marked by the launch of several new large-scale digitization efforts in Europe and Canada that aimed to correct the Anglo-American skew of the Google Print effort. 
•Ultimately, the scope and content of the “collective collection” will defined by community agreements about how aggregate institutional holdings can be most efficiently managed – some aspects, like online discovery of library-owned content, may effectively be moved to the global network level, while others, like document supply and shared print storage, remain regionally localized. 
Our goal today is to explore the impact of large-scale digital conversion on collaborative collection management – not simply to compare how different institutions are working with their respective digitization partners, but to examine how various, overlapping digitization projects are (or are not) transforming our collective ability to deliver library services to the research community.
The Internet Archive’s open access text archive provides an RSS feed to track “collection development” in real-time. 
If we look at the current state of affairs – with several, overlapping efforts projected to produce an aggregate output of more than 20M digitized titles within the next 5 years – we seem to be hastening toward a future in which massive collective investments in conversion result not in a “new Alexandria” but in a generally boggy terrain with little arable land.

Mass digitization is just one component of a larger programs and research focus on managing the collective collection. 
In this area,
•We are shaping a community discussion on the principles and “best practices” of library partnerships in mass digitization – work that will be carried forth by the ARL Committee on Scholarly Communications •We are looking at using data-mining strategies to inform selection for conversion and storage.  •We are examining the role of special collections in large-scale digitization •We are giving new thought to the role of the Registry of Digital Masters – there are multiple competing efforts or aspirations that call for a re-evaluation of how well we are meeting community expectations. •We are looking at shared print storage and the collaborative management of multi-institutional collections.
