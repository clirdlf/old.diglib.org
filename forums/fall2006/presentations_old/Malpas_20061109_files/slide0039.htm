I’m joined by four panelists who are uniquely qualified to respond to the concerns and questions raised in our survey.  [Read from list]

Each of these individuals has intimate (and in some cases rights-restricted) knowledge of the mass digitization enterprise.  We made a special effort to identify panelists involved in multiple large-scale conversion projects, who could help us to understand the different institutional motivations and interests that are served by participation in mass digitization partnerships.

Robin is managing the University of California system libraries’ participation in two of the best-known mass digitization programs:  the Open Content Alliance, which UC joined in October 2005, and Google Book Search, which UC joined in August 2006.

Sian is managing the University of Toronto’s participation in the Open Content Alliance, which the U of T joined in October 2005.  The University of Toronto is a founding member of the AlouetteCanada initiative, which officially launched in June 2006.  Sian is a member of the Technical Steering Committee for the project, which is expected to provide access to XXX million objects from libraries, archives and museums throughout Canada by 20XX

Barbara Taranto is a familiar face at DLF, where she represents the interests of the only public research library to belong to the Federation.  It’s due in large part to Barbara’s own efforts that NYPL’s digital presence rivals, if it doesn’t exceed, that of many top-ranked university and even national libraries.  NYPL was one of the first libraries to become a Google Library Partner in December 2004 and it remains the only public library in the now infamous Google 8.  In addition to the XXX titles that will be digitized through the Google Book Search program, Barbara will be facing the challenge of managing a collection of more than .5M images in NYPL’s highly-publicized and very heavily used Digital Gallery.

In addition to their ground-breaking participation in mass digitization efforts, the California Digital Library, University of Toronto and NYPL share a common bond as RLG Programs Partners – cultural heritage institutions that have identified a common need for solutions that “scale” to a higher, network level.

Our fourth and final speaker, Stuart Dempster, represents an institution of a different kind.  The UK Joint Information Steering Committee provides expert advice to higher education and research institutions and funding bodies throughout the United Kingdom.  It also manages a suite of R&D programs that are intended to provide the education sector with the tools and systems necessary to support effective and efficient use of Information and Communication Technologies in learning and research.  As manager of JISC’s Digitisation programmes, Stuart has overseen the investment of more than 16M £  (> $30M US) in cultural heritage conversion projects and has a unique vantage on scoping the aggregate outputs of mass digitization.
