Plan for Life Cycle Management: Recognize that every initiative we
undertake has multiple and ongoing phases – they are not one-time
efforts. Plan for different project phases in advance so that we can budget
accordingly and also make the right technical decisions to support
different stages of the initiative. Lifecycle management implies
institutional commitment and reinforces the connection between
development and preservation. Financial management of initiatives
should take into consideration long-term expenses, not one time creation
costs. Place equal emphasis on planning, development, management, and
maintenance stages.
2) Practice Stewardship: Assign curatorial roles and technical leads for
each initiative for accountability and ongoing stewardship to facilitate
continuing management and priority setting. We need to periodically
audit the existing collections and assess their value from various
perspectives including usability, technical stability, standards support, etc.
3) Push our Content to Where Users Are: Take into consideration
interoperability and federated access and retrieval principles. Use
standards and systems that will enable pushing content to users’ preferred
research environments, such as common search engines or portals. Add
features to enable deeper crawling of content by Google and use OAI
systematically.
4) Use the Digital Collections Registry: Record information about the
existing and new collections and services systematically. Key information
elements include collection-level definition and characterization, rights
management, delivery platforms, curatorial and technical support roles,
statistics, web services support.
