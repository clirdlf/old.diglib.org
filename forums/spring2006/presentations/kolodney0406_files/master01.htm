As Aaron mentioned, some of the keywords in the management of small and large scale projects in parallel, are consistency, structured workflows, and above all, efficiency. Keeping naming schemes consistent is sometimes a challenge; On one hand, there is a need to cater to the clients’ wish for descriptive names; on the other hand, it is essential to reduce confusion between units and across the board. The use of acronyms, common shortcuts, and library world identifiers, facilitates the main goal of making the small projects as efficient as the large ones.
In their nature, small scale projects consist of unique items or special collections, such as archival historical documents or fragile rare books – these items might demand special care and handling, full quality control, 100% OCR accuracy, and high encoding level. Processing would be linear and detailed.
In order to achieve high efficiency while performing large scale projects, one needs to compromise on quality, and at the same time, to streamline workflows; low or less-than-perfect quality is acceptable, and time-consuming processes are replaced with automated batch processes.

Moving from single, linear projects to parallel projects, and basically, to increased volume of work, had naturally imposed an increased volume of processing capabilities. We’ve moved from flatbeds to overheads and high-speed devices, and from ‘hand-made’ encoding to automated mark-up. We currently work with 1 BG network connection, and most od the automated processes are done directly on the network.
Dealing with a lot of data is not something that one would want to do in paper based environment. Although we still use some paper based documentation forms in our daily work, we move more and more towards online tracking and statistics generation. Our main tool is SharePoint, by Microsoft. Job assigning for student technicians is done via this tool, as well as listings of projects and gathering of performance statistics.
